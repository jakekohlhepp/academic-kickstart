## Why it matters

This paper sharpens the older hours-constraints argument. It asks a simple but powerful question: if workers can freely adjust hours inside a job, why should preference shifts matter so much more when they change jobs?

## Short summary

Altonji and Paxson compare how changes in labor-supply determinants translate into hours changes for workers who stay in the same job versus workers who switch jobs. They find that changes in preferences and circumstances have much larger effects on hours when a job change occurs. That pattern is hard to reconcile with a world of smooth within-job hours choice and much easier to reconcile with jobs offering constrained hours packages.

The paper matters for discrete scheduling because it treats adjustment as movement across offered schedules. Workers are not just sliding along a continuous hours margin. They are often moving between jobs that differ in wage, hours, and scheduling package.

## What to carry forward

- Job switching is evidence about hours constraints, not just worker turnover.
- Schedule choice often happens across jobs rather than within jobs.
- Measured elasticities can look small if the relevant margin is blocked inside the current job.

## Caution

The design is indirect. It infers constraints from comparative patterns rather than observing the full menu of feasible schedules. Even so, it remains one of the clearest empirical bridges from smooth labor-supply models to discrete job-package thinking.
