## Why it matters

This entry adds a Canadian tax-benefit case to the wiki. It is useful because it combines household microsimulation with general-equilibrium feedback, so the policy is not treated as a purely mechanical change in disposable income.

## Short summary

Annabi, Boudribila, and Harvey study Canada's Working Income Tax Benefit using a general equilibrium microsimulation model with nearly 30,000 families. The paper estimates labor-supply and labor-demand elasticities for calibration, then simulates changes to the benefit and related participation incentives.

The central result is policy-relevant but modest in scale: the WITB raises labor supply and GDP somewhat, while reducing low-income rates, low-income gaps, and measured inequality. The participation effects are especially important for low- and medium-skilled lone-parent families.

## What to carry forward

- Earnings supplements can work mainly through participation rather than smooth hours adjustment.
- Microsimulation is strongest here as a map of heterogeneous exposure and distributional consequences.
- General-equilibrium feedback matters because expanded labor supply can change wages and therefore the distributional effect of the policy.

## Caution

This is a calibrated policy simulation, not a quasi-experimental estimate of the WITB. It should be read with the same care as other microsimulation papers: the value comes from explicit structure and distributional detail, while credibility depends on calibration and maintained behavioral assumptions.
