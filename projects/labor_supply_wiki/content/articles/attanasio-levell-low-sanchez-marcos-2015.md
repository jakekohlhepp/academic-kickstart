## Why it matters

This paper makes a point that should sit near the center of the wiki: an aggregate elasticity is generally not a fixed structural parameter. It changes when the population, wealth distribution, employment rate, or business-cycle state changes.

## What the paper does

The authors estimate female labor-supply responses in a consistent life-cycle framework using US Consumer Expenditure Survey data. The model includes intertemporal and intratemporal choices and identifies intensive- and extensive-margin responses while allowing tastes for leisure and observed circumstances to vary across women.

## Main results

Heterogeneity is large. The median Marshallian hours elasticity is about 0.18, compared with a Hicksian elasticity of 0.54 and a Frisch elasticity of 0.87. At the 90th percentile, the corresponding estimates are 0.79, 1.16, and 1.92.

The extensive margin explains about 54 percent of the total response for women under 30, but its importance declines with age. Aggregate elasticities are also cyclical: they become larger in recessions, especially long recessions, because the distribution of people near the participation margin changes.

## What this adds to the literature

The paper connects three literatures that are too often read separately. The female labor-supply literature supplies the heterogeneity. The indivisible-labor literature supplies the importance of participation. The macro literature supplies the changing aggregate state. Together they imply that aggregation is an equilibrium calculation, not an average of coefficients.

This also sharpens the micro-macro debate. A recession can generate a different aggregate elasticity without anyone's underlying preferences changing. The weights on people and margins have changed. That is the same reason a tax reform aimed at second earners should not be calibrated with a prime-age male hours elasticity.

## Caution

The page uses the working-paper version, and the numerical distribution is model- and sample-dependent. The durable lesson is the state dependence of aggregation, not any single percentile estimate.

## What to carry forward

- Report the distribution of elasticities when heterogeneity matters.
- Separate age-specific participation from hours conditional on work.
- Treat the aggregate elasticity as a function of population weights and the economic state.
