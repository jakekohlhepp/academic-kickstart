## Why it matters

This is one of the formative papers in the early ETI literature. It helped make taxable income, rather than just hours worked, a central response variable in tax policy analysis.

## Short summary

Auten and Carroll use the 1986 U.S. tax reform as a natural experiment and study how household income changed across taxpayers facing different tax-rate changes. Their main message is not simply that taxes matter. It is that taxes matter alongside strong non-tax changes in income growth, which makes clean identification difficult.

That is exactly why the later literature keeps returning to specification, controls, mean reversion, and heterogeneity. This paper is useful less as the final word on an elasticity estimate and more as an early demonstration that taxable-income responses are policy-important but econometrically delicate.

## What to carry forward

- Taxable income is broader than classic hours-based labor supply.
- Top-income tax-response estimates are vulnerable to non-tax income trends.
- Early ETI results are best read together with later robustness work.

## Caution

Because the paper works in a setting with large contemporaneous shifts in top incomes, it is hard to cleanly separate tax effects from everything else. That is part of why later ETI papers reworked the design.
