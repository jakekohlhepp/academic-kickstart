## Why it matters

This is one of the most important papers for treating labor supply as institutionally mediated. It shows that child care systems are part of labor-supply infrastructure, not a side issue.

## Short summary

Baker, Gruber, and Milligan study Quebec's universal child care reform and find large increases in maternal labor supply. The central mechanism is straightforward: when affordable care becomes widely available, the fixed cost of market work falls and participation becomes more feasible for many mothers.

The paper matters because it pushes labor supply away from a narrow wage-hours frame. Institutions that organize care can change participation, hours, and household well-being even if wages do not move.

## What to carry forward

- Child care availability is labor-supply infrastructure.
- Participation responses often depend on fixed costs and household logistics.
- Institutions can alter labor supply through time coordination, not just cash incentives.

## Caution

This paper also documents tradeoffs in family and child outcomes. It is therefore best read as a broad institutional paper, not just a clean win-or-loss estimate for employment.
