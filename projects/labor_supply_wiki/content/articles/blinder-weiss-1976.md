## Why it matters

This is one of the early papers that makes labor supply look like a dynamic choice instead of a single-period hours decision. The core idea is that people choose work, learning, and earnings paths jointly over the life cycle.

## Short summary

Blinder and Weiss build a model in which current work effort affects both current earnings and future productivity. That means a wage change cannot always be read as a clean price signal for hours. Some of what looks like low labor supply can actually be time spent building human capital.

The paper helps explain why hours and wages evolve together over the life cycle, why younger workers may react differently than older workers, and why static labor-supply models can miss important margins of adjustment.

## What to carry forward

- Labor supply is often tied to wage growth and skill accumulation.
- A life-cycle perspective matters even before we get to econometrics.
- Later empirical papers can be read as attempts to estimate pieces of this dynamic problem more cleanly.

## Caution

This is a highly stylized theoretical paper. It is foundational because it sets the frame, not because it settles magnitudes.
