## Why it matters

This paper turned New York cabdrivers into a test of the life-cycle model. Drivers can stop working within the day, and their earnings opportunities vary from day to day. That seems like an unusually clean setting for asking whether workers move labor toward temporarily high-wage periods.

## Design and result

The authors study three samples of daily wages and hours. Earnings opportunities are correlated within a day but largely uncorrelated across days, so daily wage variation is treated as transitory. The life-cycle model predicts a positive hours response because a one-day wage change has little effect on lifetime wealth.

Instead, estimated wage elasticities are significantly negative in two of the three samples. For inexperienced drivers they average about -1 and are negative in all three samples. The authors interpret the pattern as narrow bracketing: drivers make decisions one day at a time, use a loose daily income target, and stop after reaching it.

## What made the paper important

The contribution is bigger than a negative elasticity. It offers a behavioral explanation for why field behavior might violate intertemporal substitution even where hours are flexible. Experience matters too: newer drivers look more like target earners, suggesting that learning or selection may change behavior over time.

## The thread it launched

[Farber (2005)](../articles/farber-2005-tomorrow-another-day-nyc-cabdrivers.html) argues that the negative result is sensitive to how the daily wage is constructed and that stopping is driven mainly by cumulative hours. [Farber (2008)](../articles/farber-2008-reference-dependent-preferences-taxi-drivers.html) allows a daily income reference point but finds it too unstable, with most shifts ending before the target. [Farber (2015)](../articles/farber-2015-taxi-rain-labor-supply-lessons.html) uses the full 2009-2013 trip record and finds generally positive responses to earnings opportunities.

[Fehr and Goette](../articles/fehr-goette-2007-workers-work-more-high-wages.html) add a randomized wage increase and show why the outcome measure matters: hours rise, but effort per hour falls among loss-averse workers.

## Caution

Daily earnings divided by daily hours is partly determined by the driver's stopping choice, so wage measurement and simultaneity are central. The paper is best read as the opening claim in a research program, not the final word on taxi-driver preferences.
