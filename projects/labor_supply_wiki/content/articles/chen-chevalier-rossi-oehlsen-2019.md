## Why it matters

This paper is a modern benchmark for what labor supply looks like when schedule choice is unusually fine-grained. Instead of workers selecting one fixed weekly schedule, Uber drivers can decide hour by hour whether to work.

## Short summary

Chen, Chevalier, Rossi, and Oehlsen use Uber driver data to estimate how much workers value real-time scheduling flexibility. Their key idea is that flexibility is valuable because reservation wages vary over time. When workers can choose exactly when to work, they can supply labor in low-reservation-wage hours and avoid high-reservation-wage hours.

The paper finds that Uber drivers receive much more surplus from this flexibility than they would under less flexible arrangements. For the wiki, the main lesson is comparative: the denser the schedule menu, the less severe hours mismatch should be. That makes this paper a useful counterpoint to the classic hours-constraints literature built around fixed jobs and coarse schedule options.

## What to carry forward

- Flexibility can be valued because reservation wages move over time.
- The granularity of the schedule menu changes observed labor-supply behavior.
- Platform work is useful as a contrast case to standard employer-controlled scheduling.

## Caution

Uber is not a typical employment relationship. The paper is extremely informative about the value of high-frequency schedule choice, but those estimates should not be read as the value of flexibility in ordinary jobs with teams, supervision, and coordination constraints.
