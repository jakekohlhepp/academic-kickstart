## Why it matters

This is a strong "start here" paper because it gives a disciplined way to think about frictions instead of treating them as a vague excuse. It helps explain why measured behavior may understate underlying structural responsiveness.

## Short summary

Chetty develops bounds linking observed elasticities to structural elasticities when people face optimization frictions. The idea is simple but powerful: if people do not fully optimize because adjustment is costly, confusing, delayed, or otherwise sticky, then the response seen in the data may be attenuated.

That argument matters for both positive and normative analysis. It changes how researchers interpret small tax responses, and it changes welfare analysis because frictions can themselves be part of the policy problem.

## What to carry forward

- Observed elasticity is not always the structural elasticity of interest.
- Frictions matter for both measurement and welfare.
- This paper is a bridge between quasi-experimental micro evidence and macro or sufficient-statistics reasoning.

## Caution

The paper delivers bounds, not a single universally correct elasticity. The practical interpretation still depends on assumptions about the nature and size of frictions.
