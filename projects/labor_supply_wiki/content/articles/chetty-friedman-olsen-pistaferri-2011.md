## Why it matters

This is one of the core Chetty-era papers for reconciling low measured micro elasticities with larger macro responses. It does that by taking frictions and firm-side constraints seriously.

## Short summary

Using Danish tax-record evidence, the paper argues that worker choices are shaped by adjustment costs and by hours packages that firms effectively post. When hours cannot move smoothly and workers must search for a better job package, observed responses to tax changes can look muted in the short run even if underlying preferences are more responsive.

The paper therefore shifts attention away from a simple "workers choose any number of hours they want" benchmark. Once jobs are lumpy and search is costly, the gap between micro and macro elasticities becomes easier to understand.

## What to carry forward

- Firm behavior and job design matter for measured labor-supply elasticities.
- Short-run micro responses can understate longer-run response.
- The extensive margin and search margins can carry much of the adjustment.

## Caution

This paper is tightly tied to Danish institutional detail and to a specific way of modeling adjustment costs. It is influential because it widens the mechanism set, not because it closes every identification question.
