## Why it matters

This paper asks a narrower and more useful question than whether micro and macro elasticities are generally "consistent." It asks whether the specific extensive-margin mechanism introduced by indivisible-labor models is quantitatively large enough to reconcile micro evidence with macro calibration.

## What the paper does

The authors combine two exercises. First, they use a standard calibrated macro model to translate tax changes into labor-supply responses. Second, they assemble quasi-experimental estimates of participation and employment responses. The comparison forces the macro calibration to confront the same kind of micro evidence already used to discipline intensive-margin hours responses.

## Main result

The answer depends on the macro question. For steady-state comparisons, the micro evidence is broadly compatible with the Hicksian elasticities used to study persistent tax differences across countries. The synthesis points to Hicksian elasticities around 0.3 on the intensive margin and 0.25 on the extensive margin.

For business-cycle fluctuations, the reconciliation fails. The estimated extensive-margin Frisch elasticity is around 0.25, roughly an order of magnitude below what many indivisible-labor calibrations need. Adding participation responses therefore does not, by itself, explain large cyclical movements in aggregate hours.

## What changed in the debate

The paper splits a once-blurry micro-macro puzzle into two distinct problems. Cross-country tax responses and short-run business-cycle responses require different elasticity concepts. Indivisible labor can help with the first without solving the second.

That distinction is the thread to carry into [Rogerson and Wallenius](../articles/rogerson-wallenius-2009-micro-macro-elasticities-life-cycle-taxes.html), which emphasizes life-cycle responses to persistent taxes, and [Peterman](../articles/peterman-2016-reconciling-micro-macro-frisch-elasticity.html), which argues that sample composition and extensive-margin measurement can still produce a much larger macro Frisch estimate.

## Caution

The estimates synthesize heterogeneous quasi-experiments and then map them into macro concepts. The paper is strongest as a discipline on free calibration parameters, not as a claim that every population or institutional setting shares one participation elasticity.
