## Why it matters

This is useful for the "current gaps" page because it pushes labor supply beyond the standard wage-hours frame. It asks whether occupational amenities and job satisfaction help explain why some workers look much less wage-responsive than others.

## Short summary

Using PSID data from 1968 to 2017, Dastan and Farhidi estimate heterogeneity in Frisch elasticities across occupations and industries. They argue that workers in occupations with stronger intrinsic satisfaction or better nonpecuniary attributes display lower elasticity, while workers in more physically demanding or less attractive occupations display higher elasticity.

Even if you do not take every estimate at face value, the paper points to a live frontier question: how much apparent labor-supply heterogeneity is really about job attributes rather than just wages, demographics, or institutions?

## What to carry forward

- Occupational heterogeneity may be central, not residual.
- Nonpecuniary job attributes can alter measured wage responsiveness.
- This is a frontier area where the literature still feels thinner than the policy relevance would justify.

## Caution

This paper is very recent and more exploratory than canonical. It is best used as a signal of where the literature is moving, not as a settled benchmark.
