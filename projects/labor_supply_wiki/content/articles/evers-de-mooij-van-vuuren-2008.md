## Why it matters

This is one of the most useful bridge papers for your new sections because it speaks to both demographic heterogeneity and method credibility. It is not trying to produce one magic elasticity. It is trying to explain why the literature looks so dispersed.

## Short summary

Evers, de Mooij, and van Vuuren conduct a meta-analysis of uncompensated labor-supply elasticities. Their synthesis suggests that elasticities for men are generally small, while elasticities for women are larger, though still variable across settings. They also show that reported elasticities depend on modeling choices such as the specification of the hours equation and the estimation method.

In plain terms, this paper says two things at once. Part of the variation in the literature is real economic heterogeneity across populations. Another part is methodological heterogeneity created by how researchers set up the estimation problem.

## What to carry forward

- Men and women do not generally look equally elastic in the empirical literature.
- Method differences are part of the reason estimates differ.
- Review and meta-analysis work is essential for calibration, even when it is not an identification design by itself.

## Caution

A synthesis is only as good as the underlying studies it aggregates. This paper is valuable because it organizes the literature, not because it eliminates all concerns about comparability.
