## Why it matters

Farber takes the behavioral hypothesis seriously enough to estimate it directly. Rather than treating any negative hours-wage relationship as proof of income targeting, the paper asks whether a daily income reference point has stable, observable consequences for when drivers stop.

## What the paper finds

A daily income reference level may affect labor supply, but the estimated reference point varies substantially from day to day for the same driver. More damaging for a strong target-earning interpretation, most shifts end before the driver reaches the estimated reference income.

The data therefore do not support an important role for reference-dependent income preferences in daily stopping. The paper does not claim that reference points never matter. It shows that a useful reference-dependent model must explain what sets the target, why it moves, and why drivers so often stop short of it.

## What this adds to the thread

The paper separates two claims that were bundled together after [Camerer et al.](../articles/camerer-babcock-loewenstein-thaler-1997-nyc-cabdrivers.html): that measured wage elasticities can be negative, and that daily income targeting causes the negative relationship. [Farber (2005)](../articles/farber-2005-tomorrow-another-day-nyc-cabdrivers.html) attacks the measurement of the elasticity. This paper attacks the behavioral interpretation on its own terms.

[Fehr and Goette](../articles/fehr-goette-2007-workers-work-more-high-wages.html) provide an illuminating middle position. They find positive total labor-supply responses to a randomized wage increase, but loss-averse workers reduce effort within shifts. Reference dependence can matter without generating a negative response in total labor supplied.

## Caution

Reference points may be expectations-based, multidimensional, or attached to hours as well as income. A model with a single daily income target may reject a particular formulation rather than the broader class of reference-dependent preferences.
