## Why it matters

Farber revisits the taxi debate with the complete record of NYC taxi trips from 2009 through 2013. The scale changes what can be learned: average responses, driver heterogeneity, experience, and exit can be studied together instead of inferred from a few driver samples.

## Main results

Drivers generally respond positively to both anticipated and unanticipated increases in earnings opportunities. In a stopping model, cumulative hours strongly increase the probability that a shift ends, while cumulative income is weakly related at best. Estimated labor-supply elasticities vary across drivers, but they are usually positive and rarely substantially negative.

Experience adds a new mechanism to the literature. New drivers with smaller elasticities are more likely to leave the industry. Drivers who remain quickly display more positive responses as they gain experience. The early finding that inexperienced drivers look more like target earners can therefore reflect both learning and selection.

## What resolves and what remains

Relative to [Camerer et al.](../articles/camerer-babcock-loewenstein-thaler-1997-nyc-cabdrivers.html), the paper strongly favors the neoclassical prediction for total labor supplied. Relative to [Farber (2005)](../articles/farber-2005-tomorrow-another-day-nyc-cabdrivers.html), it confirms the importance of hours in the stopping decision with much richer data. Relative to [Farber (2008)](../articles/farber-2008-reference-dependent-preferences-taxi-drivers.html), it further weakens the case for daily income targeting as the main explanation.

But the broader behavioral question remains open. [Fehr and Goette](../articles/fehr-goette-2007-workers-work-more-high-wages.html) show that effort per hour can still fall among loss-averse workers even when total labor rises. Later work can therefore move beyond "target earner or optimizer" and ask which component of labor supply responds.

## Bridge to platforms

Uber retains flexible timing but adds algorithmic pricing, dispatch, and nearly continuous entry. The taxi literature supplies the identification warnings that platform studies inherit: realized wages are endogenous, workers select into active periods, and experience changes both behavior and sample composition.

## Caution

Large administrative data solve sampling problems but do not automatically create exogenous wage variation. The paper's strength is the combination of scale, stopping models, and heterogeneity analysis rather than a randomized wage design.
