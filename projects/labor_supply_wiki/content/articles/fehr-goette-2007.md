## Why it matters

Fehr and Goette supply a rare experimental answer to a classic intertemporal labor-supply question. The setting lets workers choose both how many shifts to work and how much effort to exert within a shift, so the paper can separate hours from productivity.

## Design

The field experiment studies bicycle messengers in Zurich who are paid on commission and can choose their five-hour shifts. The experiment temporarily raises the commission rate by 25 percent. Because the wage increase is randomized and short-lived, it avoids the usual problem that high observed wages may reflect demand conditions that also affect willingness to work.

## Main results

Workers supply substantially more labor when the commission rate is high. The estimated elasticity of overall labor supply is about 1.12-1.25, and the hours elasticity is even larger at 1.34-1.50. But effort per shift falls by roughly 6 percent, an effort elasticity near -0.24.

That combination matters. The standard intertemporally separable model can explain working more shifts when the wage is temporarily high, but it does not naturally explain lower effort within each shift. A companion experiment shows that loss-averse workers drive the negative effort response, while workers who do not display loss aversion do not significantly reduce effort.

## How it changes the taxi debate

The cabdriver literature often treats a negative daily hours-wage relationship as evidence of income targeting or reference dependence. Fehr and Goette show why that interpretation is incomplete. Participation, hours, and effort can move in different directions. Looking only at hours or only at revenue can therefore misclassify the underlying response.

The experiment also gives the neoclassical model a partial victory: overall labor supply responds positively and strongly to a temporary wage increase. Behavioral forces matter for effort composition, but they do not overturn intertemporal substitution in total labor supplied.

## Caution

The workers have unusually strong control over shifts and are paid on commission. That makes the design clean but limits direct extrapolation to salaried workers with employer-set schedules. The loss-aversion evidence also points to preference heterogeneity rather than one universal behavioral model.

## What to carry forward

- Separate participation, hours, and effort whenever the data permit.
- Temporary wage responses are easier to interpret when wage variation is experimentally assigned.
- Reference dependence can shape the composition of labor supply without making total labor supply backward bending.
