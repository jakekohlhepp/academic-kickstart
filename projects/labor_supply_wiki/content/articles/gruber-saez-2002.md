## Why it matters

If Feldstein is the provocative starting point, Gruber and Saez are a central follow-up. This paper became a benchmark because it tried to clean up identification while still keeping the taxable-income object.

## Short summary

Gruber and Saez use a longer tax panel and multiple reforms from the 1980s to estimate taxable-income responses. They find an overall ETI around 0.4, with substantially larger responses among higher-income taxpayers. They also estimate income effects and conclude that they are relatively small in their setting, which brings compensated and uncompensated taxable-income elasticities close together.

That final point makes this paper especially relevant for the new Graber et al. paper. Gruber and Saez effectively show what you can learn if you are willing to make stronger assumptions about how income and substitution effects behave. Graber et al. revisit those assumptions directly.

## What to carry forward

- ETI estimates can vary meaningfully across the income distribution.
- Income effects matter for interpretation even when they look small empirically.
- Moderate ETI estimates can still imply important policy effects.

## Caution

The paper is methodologically influential, but some of its identifying assumptions and its treatment of heterogeneity are exactly what later papers challenge.
