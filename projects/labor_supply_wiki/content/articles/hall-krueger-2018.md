## Why it matters

Hall and Krueger provide the descriptive baseline for the platform labor-supply literature. Before asking how elastic drivers are or how much they value flexibility, the field needed to know who drives, whether platform work replaces a job or supplements one, and how freely hours actually move.

## What the paper does

The paper combines Uber administrative records with two driver surveys. Drivers look more like the general workforce in age and education than like conventional taxi drivers. Most had full- or part-time employment before joining Uber, and many retained those jobs while driving. Hours vary substantially from week to week, and earnings per hour do not fall sharply with hours worked.

Drivers report several motives that fit together: additional income, control over schedules, work-family flexibility, and smoothing unstable income from other sources. Platform work therefore appears less like a single occupation and more like a margin workers can open and close around other commitments.

## What this contributes to the thread

The paper turns flexibility from a slogan into an institutional fact, but it does not yet identify its value. [Mas and Pallais](../articles/mas-pallais-2017-valuing-alternative-work-arrangements.html) show experimentally that schedule attributes have heterogeneous values and that employer discretion can be especially costly. [Chen et al.](../articles/chen-ding-list-mogstad-2025-job-flexibility.html) then use experimental wage variation and high-frequency choices to recover reservation wages, fixed shift-entry costs, and dynamic flexibility values within Uber.

The connection to the older taxi literature is equally important. Taxi papers used flexible stopping to study temporary wage responses. Uber adds a denser schedule menu, app-based pricing, and a two-sided market, so labor supply and platform equilibrium become harder to separate.

## Caution

This is descriptive evidence produced with proprietary platform data. One author worked for Uber and the other consulted for the company. Selection into driving is also central: workers who value flexibility are more likely to join. The paper establishes the composition and use of the platform, not a causal valuation of flexibility or a complete accounting of driver costs.

## What to carry forward

- Platform work often supplements rather than replaces standard employment.
- Fine schedule control creates a labor-supply margin that conventional jobs suppress.
- Descriptive platform data are essential context, but experiments are needed to separate selection from valuation.
