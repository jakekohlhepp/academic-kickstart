## Why it matters

This paper is a reminder that tax policy hits households, not isolated workers. It is one of the classic family-labor-supply papers connecting nonlinear tax schedules to joint labor-supply decisions inside the household.

## Short summary

Hausman and Ruud estimate a family labor-supply model with both federal and state taxation. A key contribution is methodological: the paper handles the budget set created by taxes while also accounting for the fact that a nonworking spouse still has an economically relevant potential wage.

Substantively, the paper points toward stronger responsiveness for wives than for husbands and uses the model to discuss efficiency costs and policy changes such as relief from the marriage tax.

## What to carry forward

- Taxes matter through the shape of the budget set, not just the average tax rate.
- Family labor supply is a joint choice problem.
- The literature on second earners and participation incentives grows naturally out of this setup.

## Caution

The model is structural and tied to the institutional details of a specific U.S. tax environment. It is foundational, but it should not be read as a transportable elasticity estimate for every setting.
