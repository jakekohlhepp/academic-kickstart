## Why it matters

This is one of the classic warnings against the unconstrained-hours assumption. It makes a very simple but important point: if workers cannot freely vary hours within jobs, then actual hours are a noisy stand-in for desired hours.

## Short summary

Kahn and Lang use Canadian data on desired and actual hours to show that labor-supply estimates based on actual hours can be biased upward when workers face hours constraints. The paper is concise, but it has had a lasting impact because it gives a direct empirical critique of a standard modeling assumption.

For the wiki, this is a key link between the old hours-constraint literature and later Mas-Pallais style work. Both are really about the limits of treating observed hours as unconstrained choice.

## What to carry forward

- Desired hours and actual hours are not the same object.
- Ignoring within-job hours constraints can bias elasticity estimates.
- Even small papers can be foundational when they attack a central assumption cleanly.

## Caution

The paper emphasizes bias direction and conceptual importance more than a fully general model of constrained choice. It is best paired with broader structural or experimental work.
