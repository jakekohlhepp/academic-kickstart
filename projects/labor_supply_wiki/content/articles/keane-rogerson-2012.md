## Why it matters

This short survey is a direct challenge to a common reading of the older micro literature: that small hours responses for prime-age workers imply small aggregate labor-supply responses. Keane and Rogerson argue that the conclusion depends on a model that excludes precisely the margins that matter for many macro and tax questions.

## The argument

The paper revisits the simple life-cycle models behind the conventional wisdom and asks what happens when two empirically important features are added. First, current work can accumulate human capital and raise future wages. Second, labor supply can adjust through employment as well as hours conditional on work.

Both extensions break the tight mapping from a micro hours regression to an aggregate elasticity. Human capital means a wage-hours relationship can mix substitution with investment in future earnings. The extensive margin means aggregate hours can move even if employed workers barely change their schedules.

## What changed in the literature

The paper's strongest claim is not that one large elasticity replaces one small elasticity. It is that the old micro estimate is not a sufficient statistic for aggregate response. Policy effects must be derived from a model that contains the relevant state variables and margins.

That puts the paper between two neighboring approaches. [Chetty](../articles/chetty-2012-bounds-on-elasticities.html) keeps a simpler structural object but shows how optimization frictions attenuate observed responses. Keane and Rogerson instead expand the behavioral model itself. [Kleven et al.](../articles/kleven-kreiner-larsen-sogaard-2025-dynamic-returns-to-effort.html) later supply quasi-experimental evidence for one of the dynamic mechanisms: long-run earnings responses mediated by job switching.

## Caution

Showing that small micro estimates are consistent with large aggregate effects is not the same as identifying the aggregate effect. The richer model weakens the old negative conclusion, but it also increases the number of mechanisms that must be disciplined with data.
