## Why it matters

This survey provides the cleanest statement of a principle that runs through the whole wiki: a labor-supply elasticity is usually an outcome of a model and an experiment, not a primitive number that can be moved unchanged from one setting to another.

## The organizing claim

A labor-supply response depends on preferences, technology, market structure, and the shock being studied. A temporary wage change, a permanent tax reform, and a business-cycle fluctuation need not reveal the same object. In a dynamic model, labor supply is also a path over ages, not one hours number.

Keane and Rogerson organize the structural reconciliation around three mechanisms. Human-capital accumulation can make standard wage-hours regressions understate the relevant preference response. Aggregate adjustment can occur through employment as well as hours per worker. Structural models with an extensive margin often produce large aggregate responses even when they fit micro behavior.

## How it connects competing camps

The paper helps clarify why the literature is not simply divided into "small micro" and "large macro" camps. [Rogerson and Wallenius](../articles/rogerson-wallenius-2009-micro-macro-elasticities-life-cycle-taxes.html) show one life-cycle aggregation mechanism. [Chetty](../articles/chetty-2012-bounds-on-elasticities.html) and [Chetty et al.](../articles/chetty-friedman-olsen-pistaferri-2011-adjustment-costs.html) emphasize optimization and job-search frictions. [Chetty, Guren, Manoli, and Weber](../articles/chetty-guren-manoli-weber-2013-indivisible-labor-meta-analysis.html) use quasi-experimental evidence to discipline the extensive margin.

These approaches answer different questions. Structural models explain how multiple margins combine in counterfactuals. Friction-based papers explain why observed local responses can be attenuated. Meta-analysis asks whether the magnitudes required by calibration are present in the data.

## What to carry forward

- Define the shock before choosing the elasticity.
- Treat extensive-margin and human-capital channels as mechanisms to be measured, not automatic reconciliation devices.
- Use micro estimates to discipline structural parameters, but do not assume a local estimate is the aggregate policy response.

## Caution

The survey makes a persuasive case that older small estimates do not settle aggregate questions. Its preferred interpretation still depends on structural models whose extra mechanisms require their own evidence. Reconciliation expands what is possible; it does not eliminate model uncertainty.
