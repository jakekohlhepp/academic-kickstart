## Why it matters

This is one of the strongest later ETI papers for readers who want cleaner identification than the early U.S. reform studies could usually deliver.

## Short summary

Kleven and Schultz use Danish administrative data spanning multiple reforms and decades. Their design is valuable because the tax variation is rich and the data make it easier to address concerns about mean reversion, inequality trends, and fragile specification choices that haunted parts of the earlier ETI literature.

The paper is especially useful as a middle point between classic ETI studies and newer work like Graber et al. It shows what a stronger reform-based design can recover, while still leaving open the later question of how far taxable-income responses can be mapped into underlying labor-supply substitution and income effects.

## What to carry forward

- Administrative data and repeated reforms can materially improve ETI credibility.
- Graphical and nonparametric evidence matter in this literature.
- Better causal identification still does not eliminate interpretation questions about the underlying elasticity object.

## Caution

The setting is Denmark, with institutions and data quality that do not automatically generalize. The paper improves causal credibility but still leaves open broader external-validity questions.
