## Why it matters

This paper is a strong reminder that labor supply is embedded in care systems. When caregiving needs rise, labor supply can fall sharply even without any change in wages or taxes.

## Short summary

Maestas, Messel, and Truskinovsky use administrative data to estimate how caregiving affects labor supply. They find substantial reductions in work linked to caregiving responsibilities, with important persistence over time. The paper shows that family care demands create real labor-supply constraints rather than small transitory inconveniences.

For this wiki, the paper belongs with institution-focused work because it highlights what happens when care has to be privately absorbed. The weaker the surrounding support system, the more caregiving becomes a labor-supply shock.

## What to carry forward

- Care responsibilities can create large and persistent labor-supply effects.
- Family constraints are central, not secondary, for many workers.
- Administrative data are especially valuable for tracing dynamic caregiving effects.

## Caution

This paper identifies the effect of caregiving burdens more directly than it identifies which exact institutional reform would offset them. It is therefore strongest as a constraint-mapping paper.
