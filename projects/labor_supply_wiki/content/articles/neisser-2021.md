## Why it matters

This is the ETI literature's calibration page. Instead of asking which single study is right, Neisser asks why the whole distribution of estimates looks the way it does.

## Short summary

Using a large database of published ETI and broad-income elasticity estimates, Neisser shows that reported elasticities are correlated with specification choices, country context, and how the tax base is defined. The paper also finds evidence of selective reporting bias.

This is a high-value addition to the wiki because it helps prevent over-reading any one ETI estimate. It also reinforces the practical message of the new Graber paper: the object being estimated and the assumptions behind the estimator matter at least as much as the headline coefficient.

## What to carry forward

- ETI estimates are not fixed constants across designs and settings.
- Tax-base definition and specification choices shift results materially.
- Meta evidence is useful for benchmarking, not just for averaging.

## Caution

Meta-regression can tell you why the literature varies, but it cannot substitute for understanding the best underlying designs. Use it to discipline interpretation, not to skip reading the core papers.
