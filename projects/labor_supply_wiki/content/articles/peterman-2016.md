## Why it matters

Peterman makes the micro-macro comparison concrete by rebuilding the micro estimate until it resembles the population and margins represented in a macro model. The exercise shows that part of the famous gap is created by comparing estimates with different denominators.

## What the paper does

The standard micro Frisch estimate is usually based on prime-age married male household heads who are already working, so it captures only intensive-margin hours changes for a selected population. Peterman broadens the sample to include women, single people, secondary earners, younger workers, and older workers, and then adds extensive-margin movements into and out of employment.

## Main result

The benchmark micro estimate is around 0.2. Once the population and margin restrictions are relaxed, the estimate rises to roughly 2.9-3.1, in the range often used in macro calibration. Secondary earners and the participation behavior of younger and older workers are especially important.

The large number is not the end of the story. It is sensitive to the estimation procedure and to whether older workers are included. Retirement behavior can raise the aggregate response sharply, so the appropriate calibration depends on whether retirement is part of the model's target variation.

## How it changes the thread

[Chetty, Guren, Manoli, and Weber](../articles/chetty-guren-manoli-weber-2013-indivisible-labor-meta-analysis.html) conclude that measured extensive-margin Frisch responses are too small to explain common business-cycle calibrations. Peterman reaches a much larger estimate by changing the sample and constructing a macro-consistent object from PSID data. The disagreement is therefore partly methodological: separate quasi-experimental elasticities versus a pseudo-panel estimate designed to mimic aggregate variation.

This makes the paper a useful bridge to [robust Frisch inference](../articles/keane-neal-2025-robust-inference-frisch-labor-supply-elasticity.html). Once sample composition and participation can move an estimate this much, uncertainty about the object is at least as important as its standard error.

## Caution

The paper demonstrates that a macro-consistent estimate can be large, not that 3.0 is a universal structural parameter. Composition, retirement, and the treatment of nonworkers are doing substantive economic work.
