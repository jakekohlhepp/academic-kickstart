## Why it matters

Rogerson changes the unit of analysis. At the individual level, work is indivisible: a person either takes a fixed work package or does not work. At the aggregate level, however, employment can vary smoothly because different people move into and out of work. That move makes a large aggregate labor-supply response possible without requiring every employed worker to adjust hours sharply.

## What the model does

The paper studies an economy with identical agents and indivisible labor. The planner can use lotteries over employment and consumption, so the discontinuous individual choice is effectively smoothed through aggregation. The result is not that literal lotteries are a labor-market institution. The lottery is a device for representing who works when identical people face a nonconvex work choice.

## What changed in the literature

Before this paper, it was natural to map aggregate hours directly to the intensive-margin hours choice of a representative worker. Rogerson shows why that mapping can fail. Aggregate hours can be highly responsive because the employment rate moves, even when hours conditional on employment barely move.

That insight became a foundation of macro labor-supply models. It also created the empirical question taken up by [Chetty, Guren, Manoli, and Weber](../articles/chetty-guren-manoli-weber-2013-indivisible-labor-meta-analysis.html): are actual extensive-margin responses large enough to support the calibrations built on the indivisible-labor mechanism?

## The deeper connection

The same nonconvexity appears in the hours-constraints literature in a less abstract form. [Dickens and Lundberg](../articles/dickens-lundberg-1993-hours-restrictions-and-labor-supply.html) model workers choosing among jobs with fixed hours, while [Mas and Pallais](../articles/mas-pallais-2017-valuing-alternative-work-arrangements.html) study how workers value schedule bundles. Rogerson is therefore the macro ancestor of the modern claim that workers often choose packages rather than a smooth hours dial.

## What not to infer

The paper establishes a mechanism, not its empirical magnitude. Whether indivisible labor explains business-cycle hours, cross-country differences, or a particular tax reform depends on the distribution of reservation wages and on which extensive margins actually move.
