## Why it matters

Rogerson and Wallenius show that a small elasticity estimated from prime-age workers need not be informative about the aggregate response to a persistent tax change. The reason is not statistical noise. The micro regression and the macro counterfactual move different parts of the life-cycle work profile.

## What the model does

The paper builds an overlapping-generations life-cycle model with both intensive and extensive labor-supply margins. It can reproduce a standard micro estimation exercise using wage and hours variation among prime-age workers, then use the same underlying preferences to compare steady states with different tax and transfer systems.

## Main result

Tax changes produce large effects on aggregate hours even when the model generates small prime-age micro elasticities. In the model, the two elasticities are effectively unrelated because persistent taxes can change whether people work, when they enter or leave employment, and how hours are distributed over the life cycle. A prime-age hours regression sees only a narrow slice of that adjustment.

## What this adds to the thread

[MacCurdy](../articles/macurdy-1981-life-cycle-labor-supply.html) made intertemporal substitution measurable inside a life-cycle model. Rogerson and Wallenius push the same structure toward an aggregate tax question and add the extensive margin. [Keane and Rogerson](../articles/keane-rogerson-2012-micro-macro-labor-supply-elasticities.html) then generalize the argument: once human capital and participation are admitted, the old conventional mapping from micro estimates to aggregate responses breaks down.

The important contrast is with [Chetty, Guren, Manoli, and Weber](../articles/chetty-guren-manoli-weber-2013-indivisible-labor-meta-analysis.html). Rogerson and Wallenius establish that a life-cycle mechanism can reconcile the objects; Chetty and coauthors ask whether measured extensive-margin responses are large enough to support common calibrations.

## Caution

This is a structural reconciliation. Its force comes from making lifetime margins explicit, but the magnitude of the tax response depends on the model's participation technology, wage profile, transfers, and calibration. The paper shows why a small micro estimate is not dispositive; it does not make every large macro elasticity credible.
