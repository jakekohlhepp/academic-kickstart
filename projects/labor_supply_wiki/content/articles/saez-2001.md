## Why it matters

Saez makes optimal tax theory usable by empirical public finance. Instead of leaving the Mirrlees problem in terms of utility-function parameters, the paper rewrites the central formulas using behavioral elasticities and the observed income distribution.

## What the paper does

The paper derives optimal nonlinear income-tax formulas using compensated and uncompensated earnings elasticities. For top incomes, the formula becomes especially transparent: the optimal rate depends on behavioral responsiveness and the thickness of the top tail. In the general schedule, the income distribution and social welfare weights determine where redistribution is valuable, while elasticities determine how much the tax base erodes.

## What changed in the literature

This is a bridge from structural optimal-tax theory to sufficient-statistics public finance. The contribution is not merely a new formula. It changes what evidence the theory asks empiricists to produce. A tax-policy debate can be organized around a small set of estimable objects instead of a fully specified utility function.

That success also creates the next literature's problem: which empirical elasticity belongs in the formula? [Feldstein](../articles/feldstein-1995-effect-of-marginal-tax-rates-on-taxable-income.html) proposes taxable income as a broad response measure. [Gruber and Saez](../articles/gruber-saez-2002-elasticity-of-taxable-income.html) estimate it across reforms and income groups. [Saez, Slemrod, and Giertz](../articles/saez-slemrod-giertz-2012-eti-critical-review.html) emphasize avoidance, timing, and the tax base. [Graber et al.](../articles/graber-havarstein-mogstad-torsvik-vestad-2026-substitution-income-effects-taxation.html) return to the deeper question of when ETI identifies compensated and uncompensated labor-supply responses.

## The important connection

The formula does not make all response margins equivalent. Hours, effort, reporting, deductions, and shifting can have similar revenue effects but different welfare implications and different sensitivity to enforcement. The sufficient statistic is sufficient only under the model and policy environment that justify it.

## Caution

Optimal rates are not determined by an elasticity alone. They also depend on the income distribution, social welfare weights, and how tax revenue is valued. Treating the formula as a mechanical revenue-maximizing rule strips out the redistributive problem the paper was designed to clarify.
