## Why this page belongs in a labor-supply wiki

Some of your model context is about assignment rather than a standard hours choice. When workers, firms, tasks, and job attributes are heterogeneous, labor supply is partly about who matches with whom.

That matters because the labor-supply curve observed by a firm is not only a preference object. It is also a sorting object. Different workers accept different bundles, firms design different jobs, and market outcomes depend on how those two distributions meet.

## What assignment adds

Assignment models make it natural to ask whether an observed wage or hours outcome is caused by preferences, productivity, search frictions, or sorting. That is exactly the ambiguity running through compensating differentials, monopsony, and organizational labor supply.

## Why it is becoming more useful

The newer parts of the wiki care about job design: flexibility, remote work, task content, career ladders, outside options, and platform rules. Those are not just amenities added to a wage. They are dimensions of matching. A worker's labor supply response to a wage offer may depend on whether the offer is attached to the right schedule, manager, commute, task mix, or promotion path.

## Link to the rest of the wiki

Assignment and matching are the theory layer underneath compensating differentials, occupational preferences, monopsony, and organizational aggregation.

## Practical use

Use this page when a standard elasticity feels under-specified. If workers and jobs are multidimensional, the policy-relevant question may be less "how many hours at this wage?" and more "which workers can be matched to which bundles at which compensation?"
