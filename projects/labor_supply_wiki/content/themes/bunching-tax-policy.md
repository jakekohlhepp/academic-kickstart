## Why this page exists

Bunching and tax-reform designs are attractive because policy schedules create sharp incentives. But these estimates often mix real labor-supply responses, reporting, timing, knowledge, and optimization frictions.

The interesting part is that bunching turns the tax code into a diagnostic device. A kink or notch reveals whether taxpayers notice incentives, can adjust, and have an available margin of response. When the response is missing, the absence is also informative: it can point to frictions, limited knowledge, coarse choice sets, or a margin that is simply not worth moving.

## Reading path

Start with [Bounds on Elasticities With Optimization Frictions](../articles/chetty-2012-bounds-on-elasticities.html), then read [Using Notches to Uncover Optimization Frictions and Structural Elasticities](../articles/kleven-waseem-2013-notches-optimization-frictions-structural-elasticities.html). Use [Using Differences in Knowledge across Neighborhoods to Uncover the Impacts of the EITC on Earnings](../articles/chetty-friedman-saez-2013-eitc-knowledge-neighborhoods-earnings.html) for salience and knowledge, and [Tax Reforms and Intertemporal Shifting of Wage Income](../articles/kreiner-leth-petersen-skov-2016-danish-tax-reform-micro-elasticities.html) for timing responses.

For the larger ETI frame, keep [The Elasticity of Taxable Income with Respect to Marginal Tax Rates](../articles/saez-slemrod-giertz-2012-eti-critical-review.html), [Estimating Taxable Income Responses Using Danish Tax Reforms](../articles/kleven-schultz-2014-estimating-taxable-income-responses-danish-tax-reforms.html), and [Tax Reforms and Intertemporal Shifting of Wage Income](../articles/kreiner-leth-petersen-skov-2016-danish-tax-reform-micro-elasticities.html) together. They make the same warning from different angles: taxable income is a broad response object, not automatically hours worked.

## What bunching is especially good at

Bunching is useful when the question is local and the incentive is visible. It can show that taxpayers understand a threshold, that adjustment costs are small enough to act, or that a policy is salient enough to change earnings. This is why [Using Notches to Uncover Optimization Frictions and Structural Elasticities](../articles/kleven-waseem-2013-notches-optimization-frictions-structural-elasticities.html) belongs next to [Bounds on Elasticities With Optimization Frictions](../articles/chetty-2012-bounds-on-elasticities.html): one looks at the behavioral pile-up, while the other asks what a muted pile-up implies about the underlying elasticity.

## What it is bad at by itself

A bunching estimate often cannot tell whether adjustment came from hours, effort, deductions, reporting, avoidance, or timing. That is why this page should be read with [taxable income, substitution, and income effects](../themes/taxable-income-and-income-effects.html), where lottery papers and the newer Graber et al. entry help separate income effects from substitution-style tax responses.

## Practical warning

A sharp policy design can identify a sharp local response while still leaving open whether the response is hours, effort, reporting, or timing.
