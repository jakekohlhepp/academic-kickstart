## Why this page matters

If jobs are bundles, a wage elasticity alone can be misleading. Workers may trade pay for flexibility, predictability, remote work, task content, or other amenities.

This page is where labor supply stops looking like a response to a scalar wage and starts looking like a choice among job bundles. That distinction matters because a low wage response can mean workers do not care much about money, but it can also mean that pay is bundled with flexibility, autonomy, commuting costs, or schedule stability.

## The key interpretation move

The wage is often a residual price after amenities have already been chosen. If one firm offers a rigid schedule at higher pay and another offers flexibility at lower pay, the observed wage gap is not enough to infer preferences without modeling the bundle. That is why this page belongs beside [hours mismatch and alternative work arrangements](../themes/hours-mismatch-and-work-arrangements.html).

## Why this matters for heterogeneity

Amenities can create very different labor-supply responses across workers who have the same observed wage. Parents, caregivers, students, older workers, and workers with strong commute constraints may face different reservation wages for the same job. This links the job-attribute literature to [demographic differences in elasticities](../themes/demographic-differences-in-elasticities.html) and [care institutions and labor supply](../themes/care-institutions-and-labor-supply.html).

## Link to firm wage setting

Amenities can flatten or steepen the supply curve facing a firm. That is why this page sits next to monopsony and organization themes.
