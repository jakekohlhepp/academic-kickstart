## The short version

The labor-supply literature is strong on classic tax reforms, standard demographic groups, and canonical elasticity objects. It is still thinner than it should be on delayed adjustment, nonpecuniary job differences, organizational mediation, and the schedule granularity workers actually face.

## Gap 1: long-run adjustment is still harder to measure than short-run response

A lot of credible evidence identifies what happens soon after a tax or wage change. But [Micro versus Macro Labor Supply Elasticities: The Role of Dynamic Returns to Effort](../articles/kleven-kreiner-larsen-sogaard-2025-dynamic-returns-to-effort.html) shows why longer-run responses can be much larger when promotions, job switching, or career paths mediate adjustment.

## Gap 2: the literature still underspecifies nonpecuniary job attributes and firm organization

Standard models often act as if wage changes are doing all the work. [Occupational Preferences Heterogeneity in Labor Supply](../articles/dastan-farhidi-2026-occupational-preferences-heterogeneity.html) points toward a frontier in which amenities, job satisfaction, autonomy, and work environment help explain why seemingly similar workers display different elasticities. More broadly, the literature still has less to say than it should about how firms aggregate worker preferences through task assignment, coordination, and job design.

## Gap 3: schedule menus and timing constraints are still not integrated enough

The classic hours-constraints literature saw this clearly, but a lot of newer empirical work still reports elasticities as if workers faced a smooth choice set. [Reservation Wages and Workers' Valuation of Job Flexibility: Evidence from a Natural Field Experiment](../articles/chen-ding-list-mogstad-2025-job-flexibility.html) is a useful reminder that reservation wages and elasticities can look very different when timing choice is modeled explicitly.

## Gap 4: demographic heterogeneity is still unevenly mapped

The literature knows a lot about married women, prime-age men, and second earners. It knows much less in a systematic way about many migrant groups, caregiving burdens, disability, race and ethnicity, and how these intersect with tax-benefit design. [How Elastic is the Labour Supply of Female Migrants Relative to the Labour Supply of Female Natives?](../articles/fendel-2020-female-migrants-native-elasticities.html) is useful partly because it shows how much remains outside the old benchmark groups.

## Gap 5: incentive maps, matching frictions, and causal elasticities are still too separate

Papers like [Work incentives at the extensive and intensive margin in Europe](../articles/jara-gasior-makovec-2020-work-incentives-europe.html) are excellent at mapping the budget sets and wedges people face. But the next step, translating those wedges into causal elasticities for specific groups, is often much less complete. Recent discussion of vacancy conversion, matching efficiency, and job ladders suggests that the gap between incentive maps and realized behavior may often run through firm-side frictions.

## Bottom line

The current frontier is not mainly about producing one new headline elasticity. It is about understanding who adjusts, through which margin, over what horizon, under what job and household constraints, and within what organizational structure. The linked pages on [hours mismatch and alternative work arrangements](../themes/hours-mismatch-and-work-arrangements.html), [discrete scheduling and labor supply](../themes/discrete-scheduling-and-labor-supply.html), and [organizations and firm-side labor supply](../themes/organizations-and-firm-side-labor-supply.html) are concrete maps of that shift.
