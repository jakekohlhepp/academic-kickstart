## The basic claim

There is no single labor-supply elasticity that applies equally well to all people. The literature repeatedly finds that responsiveness differs across gender, family role, migration status, and institutional environment.

## Reading path

1. Start with [The Wage Elasticity of Labour Supply: A Synthesis of Empirical Estimates](../articles/evers-de-mooij-van-vuuren-2008-wage-elasticity-synthesis.html) for the cleanest overview of how elasticities differ across men and women and across methods.
2. Read [Changes in the Labor Supply Behavior of Married Women: 1980-2000](../articles/blau-kahn-2007-married-women-labor-supply.html) to see that even within one demographic group, elasticities can shift sharply over time.
3. Use [Family Labor Supply with Taxes](../articles/hausman-ruud-1984-family-labor-supply-with-taxes.html) to keep household interactions in view.
4. Read [How Elastic is the Labour Supply of Female Migrants Relative to the Labour Supply of Female Natives?](../articles/fendel-2020-female-migrants-native-elasticities.html) for a strong example of migrant-native heterogeneity.
5. Use [Work incentives at the extensive and intensive margin in Europe](../articles/jara-gasior-makovec-2020-work-incentives-europe.html) and [Own-Wage Labor Supply Elasticities](../articles/bargain-peichl-2016-own-wage-labor-supply-elasticities.html) to place these differences in a broader policy and review context.

## Patterns that show up repeatedly

- Prime-age men usually look less elastic than women and many secondary earners.
- Married women are not a stable benchmark; their responsiveness has changed over time.
- Household structure matters because taxes, childcare, and partner earnings affect the budget set jointly.
- Migrant-native differences can be large when labor-market integration is incomplete.
- Caregiving shocks may create persistent extensive-margin effects even when workers later re-enter through lower-hour or self-employed roles.

## What remains thin

The literature is still much richer on some groups than others. Married women, prime-age men, and second earners are heavily studied. Race and ethnicity, disability, caregiving burdens, younger cohorts, older workers aging out of employment, and many migrant subgroups are much less systematically mapped in elasticity work.
