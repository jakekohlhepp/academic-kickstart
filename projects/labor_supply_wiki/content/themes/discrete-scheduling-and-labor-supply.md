## Why this theme matters

A lot of labor-supply theory starts from a smooth hours margin. But many workers do not face that problem. They face fixed shifts, minimum-hours packages, costly job changes, or discrete moments when they can choose whether to work.

## The old insight

The classic hours-constraints literature already points in this direction.

- [Labor Supply Preferences, Hours Constraints, and Hours-Wage Trade-Offs](../articles/altonji-paxson-1988-hours-wage-tradeoffs.html) shows that wage-hours packages matter for job choice.
- [Labor Supply, Hours Constraints and Job Mobility](../articles/altonji-paxson-1992-hours-constraints-and-job-mobility.html) shows that hours adjustment often happens when workers move between jobs, not within a job.
- [The Effect of Hours Constraints on Labor Supply Estimates](../articles/kahn-lang-1991-hours-constraints-estimates.html) explains why measured elasticities are biased when desired schedules are unavailable.
- [Labor Supply with Hours Restrictions](../articles/dickens-lundberg-1993-hours-restrictions-and-labor-supply.html) puts the idea into a structural model with fixed-hours offers.

## The modern extension

Recent work keeps the same basic issue but studies it in new settings.

- [Valuing Alternative Work Arrangements](../articles/mas-pallais-2017-valuing-alternative-work-arrangements.html) shows that workers care a lot about predictable, worker-controlled schedules.
- [Labor Supply and the Value of Non-Work Time](../articles/mas-pallais-2019-value-of-non-work-time.html) shows why moving between low and high hours bundles is not a small continuous adjustment.
- [The Value of Flexible Work: Evidence from Uber Drivers](../articles/chen-chevalier-rossi-oehlsen-2019-value-of-flexible-work.html) studies a setting where workers can choose from a very dense schedule menu.
- [Reservation Wages and Workers' Valuation of Job Flexibility: Evidence from a Natural Field Experiment](../articles/chen-ding-list-mogstad-2025-job-flexibility.html) shows how dynamic modeling changes what we infer about reservation wages and elasticities when scheduling is highly flexible.

## The main connection to labor supply

Discrete scheduling changes the interpretation of elasticities. A low observed response to wages can reflect a coarse schedule menu, employer control over timing, or adjustment costs between jobs. A high response can partly reflect unusually fine schedule choice rather than unusually elastic underlying preferences.

That is why this theme belongs next to [Adjustment Costs, Firm Responses, and Micro vs. Macro Labor Supply Elasticities: Evidence from Danish Tax Records](../articles/chetty-friedman-olsen-pistaferri-2011-adjustment-costs.html). The key issue is not just preferences. It is how the feasible choice set is organized.

## Relation to hours mismatch

This page is a close companion to [hours mismatch and alternative work arrangements](../themes/hours-mismatch-and-work-arrangements.html). Hours mismatch is often the symptom. Discrete scheduling is one of the underlying mechanisms.
