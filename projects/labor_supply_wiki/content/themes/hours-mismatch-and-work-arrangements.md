## Why this theme matters

Workers often do not choose hours on a smooth margin inside a fixed job. They choose among jobs and shifts that bundle wage rates, hours levels, timing, predictability, location, and employer discretion. Hours mismatch is what we observe when those bundles do not line up with preferred schedules.

## The Mas-Pallais core

1. Start with [Valuing Alternative Work Arrangements](../articles/mas-pallais-2017-valuing-alternative-work-arrangements.html). It is the cleanest paper on worker willingness to pay for schedule control, work from home, and predictability.
2. Read [Labor Supply and the Value of Non-Work Time](../articles/mas-pallais-2019-value-of-non-work-time.html) for evidence that moving across hours bundles is not a small linear change.
3. Use [Alternative Work Arrangements](../articles/mas-pallais-2020-alternative-work-arrangements.html) as the review article that places the wider literature in one frame.

## The older backbone

The modern flexibility literature sits on an older hours-constraints foundation.

- [Labor Supply Preferences, Hours Constraints, and Hours-Wage Trade-Offs](../articles/altonji-paxson-1988-hours-wage-tradeoffs.html) shows how mismatch can appear through wage-hours trade-offs.
- [Labor Supply, Hours Constraints and Job Mobility](../articles/altonji-paxson-1992-hours-constraints-and-job-mobility.html) shows that preference changes often shift hours when workers change jobs, which is exactly what we should expect if schedules are constrained within jobs.
- [The Effect of Hours Constraints on Labor Supply Estimates](../articles/kahn-lang-1991-hours-constraints-estimates.html) shows why observed hours can bias labor-supply estimation when desired schedules are unavailable.
- [Labor Supply with Hours Restrictions](../articles/dickens-lundberg-1993-hours-restrictions-and-labor-supply.html) provides the structural benchmark in which workers choose among jobs offering fixed hours.

## Modern evidence on very flexible scheduling

The newer Uber papers help by showing the other end of the spectrum.

- [The Value of Flexible Work: Evidence from Uber Drivers](../articles/chen-chevalier-rossi-oehlsen-2019-value-of-flexible-work.html) studies a setting with extremely fine schedule choice.
- [Reservation Wages and Workers' Valuation of Job Flexibility: Evidence from a Natural Field Experiment](../articles/chen-ding-list-mogstad-2025-job-flexibility.html) adds a dynamic framework and shows how much static methods can mismeasure in that kind of environment.

Together, these papers clarify that hours mismatch is partly about the granularity of the schedule menu. Fixed weekly jobs and real-time platform work are two ends of the same labor-supply design problem.

## Why this theme belongs with organizations

This literature fits directly with [organizations and firm-side labor supply](../themes/organizations-and-firm-side-labor-supply.html). Firms aggregate worker preferences by deciding which job packages to offer, how much schedule discretion to allow, and how costly it is for workers to re-time hours.

The expanded collection also separates [platform and ridesharing labor supply](../themes/platform-labor-supply.html), [taxi driver labor supply](../themes/taxi-driver-labor-supply.html), and [compensating differentials and job attributes](../themes/compensating-differentials-and-job-attributes.html). Those pages keep the flexible-work, taxi, and job-bundle literatures from getting buried inside one broad hours-mismatch page.

## Frontier note

As of April 16, 2026, the major Mas-coauthored paper [Work Hours Mismatch](../library/pdfs/lachowska-mas-saggio-woodbury-2023-work-hours-mismatch.pdf) remains a working-paper reference in this wiki rather than a standalone article page, because article pages here are reserved for peer-reviewed research. It fits naturally into this theme because it argues that workers often have limited discretion over hours at a given employer and that mismatch is especially acute among lower-wage workers.
