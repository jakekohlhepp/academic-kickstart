## The basic idea

If work builds human capital, then current hours affect future wages. That makes the labor-supply problem dynamic even before taxes, family choices, or macro aggregation enter.

This is one of the cleanest reasons the literature keeps returning to dynamic models. A wage increase today can raise hours because leisure is more expensive, but it can also raise work because experience, promotions, and future wage paths become more valuable. In that case the observed labor-supply response is partly an investment response.

## Reading path

Start with [Life-Cycle Labor Supply with Human Capital Accumulation](../articles/shaw-1989-life-cycle-labor-supply-human-capital.html) and [Human Capital and Labor Supply: A Synthesis](../articles/blinder-weiss-1976-human-capital-and-labor-supply.html). Then move to [Intertemporal Labor Supply and Human Capital Accumulation](../articles/imai-keane-2004-intertemporal-labor-supply-human-capital.html), [Human Capital Accumulation and the Intertemporal Elasticity of Substitution of Labor](../articles/wallenius-2011-human-capital-bias-ies-labor.html), and [Labour Supply: The Roles of Human Capital and the Extensive Margin](../articles/keane-wasi-2016-human-capital-extensive-margin.html).

Read [Micro versus Macro Labor Supply Elasticities: The Role of Dynamic Returns to Effort](../articles/kleven-kreiner-larsen-sogaard-2025-dynamic-returns-to-effort.html) as the modern bridge back to the micro-macro debate. It makes the human-capital point feel less like a classic life-cycle detail and more like a general mechanism: short-run effort can matter because it changes future job ladders.

## Why it changes elasticity interpretation

An observed response to wages may mix substitution, career investment, and future wage effects. That is why human-capital papers belong next to Frisch-elasticity and tax-policy papers.

## The hidden comparison

The useful comparison is not static versus dynamic in the abstract. It is between a model where work today only creates current earnings and a model where work today changes tomorrow's opportunity set. Once the second model is plausible, tax reforms, wage shocks, and schedule constraints can all have delayed effects that a short panel may understate.
