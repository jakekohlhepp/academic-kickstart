## Why this page matters

Many macro labor-supply elasticities are large because they rely on movement into and out of work, not because every worker smoothly changes weekly hours.

Indivisible labor turns a continuous hours question into a probability or package-choice question. A worker may not be able to choose 27.4 hours, but the economy can still change aggregate hours when more people take jobs, switch schedules, or move between full-time and nonwork states.

## Reading path

[Indivisible Labor, Lotteries, and Equilibrium](../articles/rogerson-1988-indivisible-labor-lotteries-equilibrium.html) is the starting point. [Does Indivisible Labor Explain the Difference between Micro and Macro Elasticities?](../articles/chetty-guren-manoli-weber-2013-indivisible-labor-meta-analysis.html) gives the empirical extensive-margin checkpoint. [The Anatomy of the Aggregate Labor Supply Elasticity](../articles/fiorito-zanella-2012-anatomy-aggregate-labor-supply-elasticity.html), [Reconciling Micro and Macro Estimates of the Frisch Labor Supply Elasticity](../articles/peterman-2016-reconciling-micro-macro-frisch-elasticity.html), and [Towards a Micro-Founded Theory of Aggregate Labour Supply](../articles/erosa-fuster-kambourov-2016-micro-founded-aggregate-labour-supply.html) show how this enters macro measurement.

The newer [Frisch Elasticities and Indivisible Labor with Endogenous Workweek](../articles/yum-2024-frisch-elasticities-indivisible-labor-endogenous-workweek.html) is useful because it sits between the old lottery logic and modern models where the workweek itself can be shaped by institutions and job design.

## What this theme clarifies

This page is the place to separate three margins that are often bundled together:

- whether a person works at all;
- whether a worker chooses among fixed hours packages;
- whether aggregate hours move because employment probabilities change.

Those margins can all look like "labor supply," but they imply different welfare and policy objects.

## Link to scheduling

Indivisible labor is the macro cousin of the hours-constraints and discrete-scheduling theme. Both remind us that workers often choose among packages, not a smooth hours dial.

## Why it keeps reappearing

The extensive margin is one reason micro and macro estimates need not line up. It is also why care institutions, disability, retirement, and family structure matter so much: they change the fixed costs and feasibility of work, not just the slope of a smooth hours supply curve. That connects this page directly to [care institutions and labor supply](../themes/care-institutions-and-labor-supply.html), [demographic differences in elasticities](../themes/demographic-differences-in-elasticities.html), and [aggregation of heterogeneous elasticities](../themes/aggregation-heterogeneous-elasticities.html).
