## What ties these papers together

These papers treat labor supply as part of a larger dynamic problem. The worker is not just deciding today's hours. The worker, or the household, is navigating wages, taxes, future earnings, care obligations, and the timing of work over the life cycle.

## How the sequence works

- [Human Capital and Labor Supply: A Synthesis](../articles/blinder-weiss-1976-human-capital-and-labor-supply.html) gives the conceptual frame.
- [An Empirical Model of Labor Supply in a Life-Cycle Setting](../articles/macurdy-1981-life-cycle-labor-supply.html) pushes that frame into panel-data estimation.
- [Family Labor Supply with Taxes](../articles/hausman-ruud-1984-family-labor-supply-with-taxes.html) adds the household and the tax schedule.
- [Universal Child Care, Maternal Labor Supply, and Family Well-Being](../articles/baker-gruber-milligan-2008-universal-child-care.html) and [Caregiving and Labor Supply](../articles/maestas-messel-truskinovsky-2024-caregiving-and-labor-supply.html) show how the older family-labor-supply backbone extends naturally into care systems and caregiving shocks.

## Why this still matters

Even newer reduced-form and quasi-experimental papers keep inheriting questions from this older literature. Which margin is dynamic? Whose wage is relevant? How much of the response is household reallocation rather than individual hours change? How much depends on institutions that make work and care compatible? Those questions are already visible here.
