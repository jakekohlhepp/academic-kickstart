## Why this page exists

Macro labor-supply papers often need aggregate elasticities that are not the same as micro hours elasticities. This page gathers the business-cycle and cross-country references.

The macro question is usually not "does one worker change hours after a wage change?" It is whether aggregate employment and hours move enough to explain cycles, tax differences across countries, or calibrated policy experiments. That shift puts enormous pressure on participation, indivisible labor, home production, taxes, and aggregation.

## Reading path

Use [Resuscitating Real Business Cycles](../articles/king-rebelo-1999-resuscitating-real-business-cycles.html) for the RBC background. Use [Why Do Americans Work So Much More Than Europeans?](../articles/prescott-2004-americans-work-more-than-europeans.html) and [Do Taxes Explain European Employment?](../articles/ljungqvist-sargent-2006-taxes-european-employment.html) for cross-country tax arguments. Use [Reconciling Cyclical Movements in the Marginal Value of Time and the Marginal Product of Labor](../articles/hall-2009-marginal-value-time-marginal-product-labor.html) and [Hours Cyclicality and Labor Supply over the Business Cycle](../articles/beaudry-green-sand-2012-hours-cyclicality.html) for cyclical hours.

Read [Micro and Macro Elasticities in a Life Cycle Model with Taxes](../articles/rogerson-wallenius-2009-micro-macro-elasticities-life-cycle-taxes.html) as the bridge back to individual behavior. It makes clear why macro calibration cannot simply import a micro hours elasticity and call the problem solved.

## The macro tension

Many macro applications need labor supply to be fairly responsive, but many micro estimates look modest. The reconciliation can come from indivisible labor, extensive-margin movement, life-cycle reallocation, tax wedges, home production, or aggregation across heterogeneous households. Each route has different implications for welfare and policy.

## What to watch for

When a macro paper uses a large labor-supply elasticity, ask whether the response is coming from hours per worker, employment, timing over the life cycle, cross-country institutions, or business-cycle composition. Those mechanisms are not interchangeable.
