## The basic shift

Monopsony changes the labor-supply question. Instead of only asking how workers choose hours at a market wage, it asks how many workers a specific firm can attract and retain at a given wage and job bundle.

## Reading path

Start with [Monopsony in Motion](../articles/manning-2003-monopsony-in-motion.html). Then use [Firm Wage-Setting Power and Outside Options](../articles/caldwell-danieli-2019-firm-wage-setting-power.html) and [Labor Market Concentration and Employer Wage-Setting Power](../articles/dube-manning-naidu-labor-market-concentration.html) for modern firm-side wage-setting references. Pair them with [Compensating Differentials in Bundles of Job Attributes](../articles/lavetti-schmutte-2023-compensating-differentials-job-attributes.html) and [Valuing Alternative Work Arrangements](../articles/mas-pallais-2017-valuing-alternative-work-arrangements.html).

## Why it connects to organizations

The firm-facing supply curve is shaped by scheduling, amenities, task assignment, outside options, and coordination costs. It is not only a wage curve.
