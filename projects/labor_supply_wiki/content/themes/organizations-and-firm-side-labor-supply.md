## The basic claim

Organizations do not merely absorb labor supply. They aggregate it. Workers bring heterogeneous wages, reservation values, skill bundles, and preferences over flexibility, autonomy, timing, and task mix. Firms turn those inputs into realized labor demand and realized hours through internal organization.

## Task assignment is the first aggregation mechanism

One useful way to read the modern literature is that workers do not supply generic hours. They supply time that has to be matched to tasks, teams, and production structures. Firms decide whether jobs are broad or specialized, whether tasks are bundled tightly or loosely, and how much variation in worker preference they are willing to accommodate.

This perspective helps explain why [Adjustment Costs, Firm Responses, and Micro vs. Macro Labor Supply Elasticities](../articles/chetty-friedman-olsen-pistaferri-2011-adjustment-costs.html) is so important. The paper already points away from a frictionless hours-choice model and toward a world of discrete job packages.

## Scheduling systems are an organizational choice

One of the clearest ways firms aggregate worker preferences is by choosing the schedule menu itself. A firm can offer fixed weekly schedules, shift bidding, limited part-time slots, hybrid arrangements, remote work, or near-complete discretion over when to work. Those are organizational choices, not just worker-side primitives.

- [Does Working from Home Work? Evidence from a Chinese Experiment](../articles/bloom-liang-roberts-ying-2015-working-from-home.html) shows that firms can change retention and effective labor supply by redesigning where work happens.
- [Changing Workplaces to Reduce Work-Family Conflict](../articles/kelly-moen-tranby-2011-schedule-control-white-collar-organization.html) and [Changing Work and Work-Family Conflict](../articles/kelly-et-al-2014-work-family-health-network.html) show from sociology that schedule control and face-time norms are themselves part of the labor-supply environment.
- [Valuing Alternative Work Arrangements](../articles/mas-pallais-2017-valuing-alternative-work-arrangements.html) and [Reservation Wages and Workers' Valuation of Job Flexibility](../articles/chen-ding-list-mogstad-2025-job-flexibility.html) show from economics that worker valuation of these organizational choices is large and heterogeneous.

## Coordination costs create firm heterogeneity

Two firms facing the same labor market do not necessarily face the same effective labor supply curve. A firm with better coordination systems, better management, or more modular production may be able to accommodate heterogeneous worker preferences more easily than a firm with high coordination costs.

Worker heterogeneity and firm heterogeneity therefore interact. The same worker can face a different effective labor-supply problem depending on how the employer organizes schedules and tasks.

## Job amenities make the worker preference bundle multidimensional

Once job amenities are important, wages alone are not enough to summarize labor supply. [Occupational Preferences Heterogeneity in Labor Supply](../articles/dastan-farhidi-2026-occupational-preferences-heterogeneity.html) is helpful here because it treats occupations as bundles of pecuniary and nonpecuniary attributes rather than as neutral containers for hours.

Remote and hybrid work made this even harder to ignore. If flexibility itself is an amenity, then a firm may flatten the supply curve it faces by offering that amenity instead of raising wages.

## Institutions and organizations interact

Firms do not choose job packages in an institutional vacuum. Child care systems, leave rules, school schedules, and caregiving demands all affect what kinds of job packages workers can actually accept. That is why this page connects directly to [care institutions and labor supply](../themes/care-institutions-and-labor-supply.html).

## Reorganization can buffer macro shocks

When taxes, wages, or workforce composition shift, firms can respond by reorganizing tasks, schedules, and career paths instead of only moving wages or headcount. [Micro versus Macro Labor Supply Elasticities: The Role of Dynamic Returns to Effort](../articles/kleven-kreiner-larsen-sogaard-2025-dynamic-returns-to-effort.html) is useful here because it makes delayed returns, promotion channels, and job ladders part of the adjustment process.

## Why this matters for elasticity interpretation

A measured elasticity is not always a pure worker-preference object. It may also reflect task assignment, coordination frictions, the design of job packages, and the amenities embedded in employment relationships.
