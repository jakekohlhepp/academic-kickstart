## Why the last few years feel different

The recent labor-supply literature no longer looks like a simple extension of the older tax-and-hours tradition. The pandemic period first generated a wave of acute shock analysis, but the more durable shift has been structural. Recent work increasingly asks how market power, job matching, reservation wedges, caregiving shocks, and firm organization shape the labor-supply responses we observe.

## Direction 1: firm-side labor supply and monopsony are more central

A lot of recent work treats labor supply as something firms face, not just something workers choose. The main issue is no longer just whether labor supply is elastic in the aggregate. It is also whether firm-specific labor supply differs sharply from market-level labor supply because search frictions, amenities, and outside options are unevenly distributed.

This wiki still needs more dedicated article pages on the recent monopsony literature, but [Adjustment Costs, Firm Responses, and Micro vs. Macro Labor Supply Elasticities](../articles/chetty-friedman-olsen-pistaferri-2011-adjustment-costs.html), [Bounds on Elasticities With Optimization Frictions](../articles/chetty-2012-bounds-on-elasticities.html), and [Occupational Preferences Heterogeneity in Labor Supply](../articles/dastan-farhidi-2026-occupational-preferences-heterogeneity.html) already point in that direction by emphasizing job packages, frictions, and nonpecuniary job characteristics.

## Direction 1.5: newer tax papers are becoming explicit about heterogeneity and income effects

[Substitution and Income Effects of Labor Income Taxation](../articles/graber-havarstein-mogstad-torsvik-vestad-2026-substitution-income-effects-taxation.html) is a good example of a newer shift in the tax literature. Instead of treating ETI as a sufficient reduced-form summary by default, it asks which estimands are causal under heterogeneity and what outside evidence is needed to separate substitution from income effects. That feels like a broader frontier move toward sharper interpretation, not just one more elasticity estimate.

## Direction 2: matching frictions and job conversion matter more

In the early 2020s, very high vacancy-to-unemployment ratios pushed researchers to ask why posted vacancies were not converting smoothly into durable employment. That puts matching efficiency, job design, and transition frictions closer to the center of labor-supply analysis.

[Adjustment Costs, Firm Responses, and Micro vs. Macro Labor Supply Elasticities](../articles/chetty-friedman-olsen-pistaferri-2011-adjustment-costs.html) and [Micro versus Macro Labor Supply Elasticities: The Role of Dynamic Returns to Effort](../articles/kleven-kreiner-larsen-sogaard-2025-dynamic-returns-to-effort.html) are especially useful here because they make labor supply look like a search-and-reallocation problem, not just an hours problem.

## Direction 3: the extensive margin is being re-read through reservation wedges and caregiving

Recent work keeps returning to the participation decision. [Work incentives at the extensive and intensive margin in Europe](../articles/jara-gasior-makovec-2020-work-incentives-europe.html) and [Are Micro and Macro Labor Supply Elasticities Consistent?](../articles/chetty-guren-manoli-weber-2011-micro-vs-macro-elasticities.html) help frame why extensive-margin responses can be large locally while still behaving differently over bigger policy shifts.

A newer strand takes caregiving much more seriously as a labor-supply constraint. [Caregiving and Labor Supply](../articles/maestas-messel-truskinovsky-2024-caregiving-and-labor-supply.html) is especially useful because it shows with administrative data that care burdens generate persistent work reductions rather than just short-lived timing effects.

## Direction 4: schedule design and amenities are now central

Remote, hybrid, and platform work made it harder to treat amenities as a side issue. Flexible work arrangements now look like structural features of the labor supply curve faced by firms. [The Value of Flexible Work: Evidence from Uber Drivers](../articles/chen-chevalier-rossi-oehlsen-2019-value-of-flexible-work.html) and [Reservation Wages and Workers' Valuation of Job Flexibility: Evidence from a Natural Field Experiment](../articles/chen-ding-list-mogstad-2025-job-flexibility.html) are useful because they show how reservation wages and elasticities depend on the schedule menu itself.

The linked pages on [hours mismatch and alternative work arrangements](../themes/hours-mismatch-and-work-arrangements.html), [discrete scheduling and labor supply](../themes/discrete-scheduling-and-labor-supply.html), and [organizations and firm-side labor supply](../themes/organizations-and-firm-side-labor-supply.html) bring that recent shift directly into the wiki.
