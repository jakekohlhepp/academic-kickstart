## Why this page exists

The taxable-income literature and the labor-supply literature often overlap without fully lining up. ETI papers are frequently used for labor-supply policy arguments, but they are not automatically estimating compensated or uncompensated labor-supply elasticities. This page is for that gap.

## The ETI spine

Start with [The Effect of Marginal Tax Rates on Taxable Income](../articles/feldstein-1995-effect-of-marginal-tax-rates-on-taxable-income.html), [The Effect of Income Taxes on Household Income](../articles/auten-carroll-1999-effect-of-income-taxes-on-household-income.html), and [The Elasticity of Taxable Income: Evidence and Implications](../articles/gruber-saez-2002-elasticity-of-taxable-income.html). Those papers define the basic ETI debate: how strongly taxable income moves with marginal tax rates, how much of that movement is causal, and how much the answers differ across higher- and lower-income taxpayers.

[Estimating Taxable Income Responses Using Danish Tax Reforms](../articles/kleven-schultz-2014-estimating-taxable-income-responses-danish-tax-reforms.html) is a later credibility benchmark, while [The Elasticity of Taxable Income with Respect to Marginal Tax Rates: A Critical Review](../articles/saez-slemrod-giertz-2012-eti-critical-review.html) and [The Elasticity of Taxable Income: A Meta-Regression Analysis](../articles/neisser-2021-eti-meta-regression.html) tell you how to read the whole literature without mistaking one estimate for a universal parameter.

## Why the lottery papers belong here

The ETI literature is often strongest on substitution-style responses to tax wedges and weakest on separately identifying income effects. That is why lottery papers matter here. [Estimating the Effect of Unearned Income on Labor Earnings, Savings, and Consumption](../articles/imbens-rubin-sacerdote-2001-unearned-income-lottery-players.html), [The Effect of Wealth on Individual and Household Labor Supply](../articles/cesarini-lindqvist-notowidigdo-ostling-2017-wealth-and-labor-supply.html), and [Labor Supply Effects of Winning a Lottery](../articles/picchio-suetens-van-ours-2018-labor-supply-effects-winning-lottery.html) provide cleaner evidence on income effects from plausibly exogenous wealth shocks.

Those papers do not replace tax-reform evidence. They complement it by identifying a different part of the underlying labor-supply problem.

## What the new Graber paper adds

[Substitution and Income Effects of Labor Income Taxation](../articles/graber-havarstein-mogstad-torsvik-vestad-2026-substitution-income-effects-taxation.html) is the clearest current bridge across these literatures. It asks when ETI estimands are causal under heterogeneity, then combines tax-reform evidence with lottery-winnings evidence to bound or identify compensated and uncompensated labor-supply elasticities.

This is why [Estimating Taxable Income Responses with Elasticity Heterogeneity](../articles/kumar-liang-2020-eti-elasticity-heterogeneity.html), [Labor Supply and Taxes: A Survey](../articles/keane-2011-labor-supply-and-taxes-survey.html), and [Labor Supply: A Review of Alternative Approaches](../articles/blundell-macurdy-1999-labor-supply-review-alternative-approaches.html) all belong nearby. The big issue is no longer just "what is the ETI?" It is "what can ETI estimates legitimately tell us about labor supply once heterogeneity, income effects, and model structure are taken seriously?"

## More papers from the Graber literature discussion

The paper's literature section also points toward a Norway-specific bridge and a few additional complements that are worth keeping in view even where this wiki does not yet have standalone pages: Trine Engh Vattø (2020) on sluggish earnings responses, Thor O. Thoresen and Trine E. Vattø (2015) on validating discrete-choice labor-supply models against tax-response evidence, Kristoffer Berg and Thor O. Thoresen (2020) on problematic ETI response margins, Rolf Aaberge and Ugo Colombino (2018) on discrete-choice labor-supply modeling, and the lottery-income-effect papers by Bulman et al. (2021) and Golosov et al. (2024).

Those are especially useful if you want to push this cluster further toward Norwegian reform evidence, structural validation, or the newest income-effect literature.
