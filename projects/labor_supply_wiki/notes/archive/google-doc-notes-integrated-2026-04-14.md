What's the landscape of labor supply research in the last 5 years?

The landscape of labor supply research over the last five years has been defined by a transition from analyzing acute, pandemic-induced shocks to a deeper structural reevaluation of market power, extensive-margin preferences, and the mechanics of job matching. The post-2020 volatility provided a natural experiment that has accelerated several theoretical and empirical frontiers.

Here is a breakdown of the major currents in the recent literature:

1. Monopsony Power and Labor Supply Elasticities
The revitalization of monopsony frameworks has arguably been the dominant theme, moving beyond concentrated labor markets to emphasize search frictions and differentiated job amenities.

Firm-Level vs. Market-Level Elasticities: There has been a concerted effort to untangle firm-specific labor supply elasticities from aggregate elasticities. Recent structural estimations (such as those by Azar, Berry, and Marinescu) have demonstrated that failing to instrument for unobserved job characteristics yields severe downward biases. When properly identified, firm-level elasticities often sit around 4.0 to 5.0, while implied market-level elasticities hover near 0.5.

Non-Constant Elasticities: Recent frontier work, including 2025 NBER papers by Kline and others, has pushed toward relaxing the assumption of isoelastic labor supply. By mapping the distribution of workers' outside options to non-parametric shape restrictions, researchers are showing that productivity-wage passthrough and compensating differentials are highly dependent on the curvature of the outside option distribution.
+1

2. Matching Frictions and "Job Conversion"
The persistently high vacancy-to-unemployment (v/u) ratios of the early 2020s forced a reexamination of matching efficiency and the friction inherent in converting vacancies into durable employment.

Many-to-Many Matching Models: As gig-work and fractional employment expand, theoretical matching models have adapted to accommodate workers holding multiple part-time roles or fluidly moving between the formal and informal sectors.

Signal Extraction: In both developed and developing economies, recent literature emphasizes that "missing jobs" are often a conversion problem rather than a demand problem. Research is increasingly focusing on how reducing frictions—such as improving the signaling of non-cognitive skills—can shift workers into higher-productivity matches without necessarily increasing aggregate labor supply.
+1

3. The Extensive Margin and Reservation Wedges
Research into the extensive margin of labor supply has utilized new survey and administrative linkages to construct more accurate aggregate supply curves.

Reservation Raises: Papers exploring the "reservation wedge" (the ratio of a worker's reservation wage to their actual/potential wage) have revealed highly asymmetric, non-constant aggregate supply curves. Globally, these curves exhibit high local elasticities (often >3) which help explain business cycle volatility, but they display much lower arc elasticities for larger structural shifts, such as major tax holidays or policy interventions.

Caregiving and Prime-Age Participation: While prime-age (25–54) labor force participation has recovered and stabilized, the broader structural decline in LFPR is being driven by younger cohorts and the aging out of older workers. High-frequency administrative panel data has brought new clarity to how caregiving shocks persistently depress the extensive margin, particularly shifting affected workers toward lower-hour or self-employed roles if they return at all.
+1

4. Organizational Economics and Unobserved Amenities

The explosion of remote and hybrid work has forced a re-quantification of compensating differentials.

Flexible work arrangements are increasingly modeled not just as a preference, but as a structural shock to the firm's labor supply curve.

Organizational boundaries are shifting as firms face a tradeoff: offer the amenity (remote work) to flatten the labor supply curve they face, or require in-person work and pay a wage premium, thereby steepening their firm-specific supply curve and exacerbating local monopsony dynamics.

What do we know about how organizations aggregate the labor supply preferences of workers

The question of how organizations aggregate individual labor supply preferences sits at the critical intersection of organizational economics and macroeconomic labor theory. Historically, macroeconomics often treated aggregate labor supply as a simple summation of individual intensive and extensive margin decisions. However, the modern organizational perspective recognizes that firms are not passive price-takers simply absorbing hours; they are active mediators that structure, bundle, and assign tasks based on their internal capabilities and the prevailing preferences of the workforce.

When we look at how firms actually perform this aggregation, several key mechanisms emerge:

1. Task Assignment as the Primary Aggregation Mechanism
Workers do not just supply generic "hours"; they supply multidimensional skills, preferences for specific types of work, and varying reservation wages. Organizations aggregate these complex, heterogeneous inputs through internal task assignment.
Rather than a one-to-one mapping of a worker to a standardized output, firms strategically design jobs. They decide whether a worker will operate as a generalist (handling all tasks associated with a service or product) or as a specialist within a coordinated team. This firm-level matrix of task assignments is the literal engine of aggregation, translating individual worker preferences and skills into realized firm-level productivity.

2. Coordination Costs and Firm Heterogeneity
The ability to efficiently aggregate diverse labor preferences depends entirely on a firm's specific organizational capabilities.

The Cost of Specialization: Highly specialized task assignment generally increases labor productivity and output quality. However, breaking down production into specialized roles requires the firm to bear significant coordination and communication costs.
+1

Rational Inattention Frameworks: Frontier models in organizational economics often treat this as an information friction problem. The cost of deviating from a baseline "generalist" organization to a highly structured, specialized one can be modeled similarly to rational inattention (e.g., using Kullback-Leibler divergence to measure the cost of coordination). Consequently, firms with different internal coordination costs will choose entirely different organizational structures even when facing the exact same aggregate labor pool. Two workers might be complements at a highly capable firm but substitutes at a firm with high coordination costs.

3. "Population Labor Supply Effects" and Endogenous Choice Sets
Because organizations face frictions in adjusting their internal structures, they cannot costlessly tailor bespoke job offers to every single individual's preferences. Instead, firms cater their job designs and wage postings to the expected aggregate preferences of the labor market.
This creates a powerful feedback loop: an individual worker's choice set is restricted and shaped by the preferences of the broader population. If a critical mass of the labor force prefers part-time work or highly flexible arrangements, profit-maximizing firms will adjust their organizational structures to accommodate this. The firm's internal structure essentially institutionalizes the aggregate preference, altering the job landscape for all workers, regardless of their individual preferences.

4. Reorganization as a Buffer to Macroeconomic Shocks
Because organizations actively mediate labor supply, the aggregate response to economic shocks (like minimum wage hikes, tax cuts, or an influx of low-wage immigrant labor) cannot be accurately predicted without accounting for internal firm reorganization.
When labor supply preferences or compositions change, capable organizations will dynamically re-assign tasks. For instance, an influx of low-wage labor might cause highly organized firms to increase specialization—absorbing the new workers into specific roles while shifting existing workers into complementary tasks. This internal reorganization dampens the negative wage effects typically predicted by perfectly competitive models and can result in net increases in aggregate labor productivity that are invisible in standard, firm-less macroeconomic models.
