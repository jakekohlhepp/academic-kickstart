Changes in the Labor Supply Behavior of Married Women: 1980-2000

A landmark paper showing that married women's labor-supply elasticities changed sharply over time, making older elasticity estimates a poor guide to later periods.

Why it matters

This is one of the clearest papers showing that labor-supply elasticities are not fixed traits of a demographic group. Even within a classic population, married women, the relevant elasticities changed a lot over time.

Short summary

Blau and Kahn use March CPS data from 1980 to 2000 to study annual hours and wage responsiveness for married women. They find a large rightward shift in the labor-supply function in the 1980s, much less shifting in the 1990s, and a dramatic decline in both own-wage and cross-wage elasticities over the full period.

That result matters for two reasons. First, it shows that institutional and social change can alter elasticities within a demographic group. Second, it warns against recycling older married-women estimates as if they were stable structural constants. The paper is also methodologically careful, checking that the pattern survives alternative corrections for selection, taxes, measurement error, and omitted-variable concerns.

What to carry forward

  • Elasticities are historically contingent, not timeless.
  • Demographic heterogeneity also changes across cohorts and decades.
  • A group that was once highly responsive can become less responsive as attachment to the labor market strengthens.

Caution

The paper is about married women in the United States over a specific historical window. It is foundational for trend evidence, but it should not be read as a universal estimate for all women or all households.
