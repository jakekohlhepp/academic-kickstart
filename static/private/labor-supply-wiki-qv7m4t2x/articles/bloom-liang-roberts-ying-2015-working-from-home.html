Quarterly Journal of Economics - 2015

Does Working from Home Work? Evidence from a Chinese Experiment

A landmark remote-work experiment showing that organizational design changes labor supply partly by changing attrition, scheduling, and the nonpecuniary value of jobs.

Why it matters

This paper is one of the clearest demonstrations that labor supply is shaped by how firms organize work, not just by wages. The key margin is not simply hours per hour of pay. It is whether the employer offers a job package that workers can sustain.

Short summary

Bloom, Liang, Roberts, and Ying study a randomized work-from-home experiment at a Chinese call center. Working from home raised performance, reduced quit rates, and improved employee satisfaction. When the firm later allowed workers to choose, many selected remote work, indicating that job location and autonomy were meaningful parts of the employment bundle.

For this wiki, the paper matters because it shows an organizational margin of labor supply adjustment. A firm can change retention and effective labor supply by redesigning where work happens and how closely workers are monitored.

What to carry forward

  • Organizational design can shift labor supply without changing wages.
  • Remote work changes both the utility of work and the feasibility of remaining employed.
  • Retention and job sustainability are part of labor supply in a broad sense.

Caution

This is evidence from a single firm and task environment. It is best read as a clean demonstration that organizational form matters, not as a universal estimate of remote work's value.
