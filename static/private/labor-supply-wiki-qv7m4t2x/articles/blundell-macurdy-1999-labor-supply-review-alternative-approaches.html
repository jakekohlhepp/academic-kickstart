Why it matters

This is one of the best old anchor texts for reading later tax and elasticity papers. It explains why different empirical strategies often look like they disagree even when they are estimating different objects.

Short summary

Blundell and MaCurdy review the main empirical approaches to labor supply as they stood after the earlier handbook surveys. They compare difference-in-differences and natural-experiment work, nonlinear budget-constraint models, family labor-supply models, and dynamic life-cycle formulations.

For this wiki, the paper matters because it keeps asking the right clarifying question: what elasticity or behavioral margin is each design actually identifying? That framing turns out to be exactly what later ETI papers, and now the Graber et al. paper, have to revisit when they try to separate substitution effects from income effects.

What to carry forward

  • Different labor-supply models answer different policy questions.
  • Nonlinear taxes, household structure, and dynamics change the elasticity object.
  • Review pieces can be methodologically valuable, not just bibliographic.

Caution

This is a handbook review, not a single identification design. It is strongest as a map of the terrain and weaker as a source of one headline elasticity.
