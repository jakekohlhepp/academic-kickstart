Maternal Labor Supply and the Introduction of Kindergartens into American Public Schools

A classic paper showing that public kindergarten and preschool function as labor-supply institutions by changing the daily time structure faced by parents.

Why it matters

This paper is a clean example of institutions shaping labor supply through time coordination. Schooling policy is not only an education policy. It also changes whether parental work is logistically feasible.

Short summary

Cascio studies the introduction of kindergartens into American public schools and finds increases in maternal labor supply, especially for mothers whose youngest child becomes newly eligible. The key mechanism is that public schooling partially substitutes for care time during the workday, reducing a major barrier to employment.

The paper matters because it shows how labor supply responds to institutional scheduling. A school day changes the feasible set in much the same way that child care or leave policy does.

What to carry forward

  • Public schooling can operate like labor-supply infrastructure.
  • Time coordination institutions matter as much as explicit cash incentives.
  • Participation effects can be large when care constraints are binding.

Caution

The measured effect depends on the age of children and the exact school arrangements being introduced. It should not be read as a single universal preschool elasticity.
