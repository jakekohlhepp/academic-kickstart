The Effect of Wealth on Individual and Household Labor Supply: Evidence from Swedish Lotteries

A modern lottery paper showing that wealth shocks reduce earnings persistently but moderately, making it a key input for calibrating income effects rather than just asserting their existence.

Why it matters

This is one of the strongest modern lottery papers because the administrative data are rich, the design is clean, and the authors push beyond a one-period response.

Short summary

Cesarini, Lindqvist, Notowidigdo, and Östling study Swedish lottery winners and show that winning reduces earnings in a way that is immediate, persistent, and fairly similar across broad demographic groups. They then use a dynamic model to translate those earnings responses into marginal propensities to earn out of unearned income.

That makes the paper especially valuable for the new tax-and-income-effects cluster. It offers exactly the kind of external evidence on income effects that can be combined with tax-reform evidence when ETI estimates alone are not enough.

What to carry forward

  • Income effects from wealth shocks appear real but not enormous.
  • The household margin matters because spouses do not respond identically.
  • Dynamic calibration can make lottery evidence more policy-relevant.

Caution

Wealth shocks from lotteries are not identical to predictable tax changes. The paper is strongest as evidence on income effects, not as a full substitute for tax-based labor-supply evidence.
