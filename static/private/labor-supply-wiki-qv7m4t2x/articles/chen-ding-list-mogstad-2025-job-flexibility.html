Why it matters

This paper extends the Uber flexibility literature from a headline valuation result to a richer dynamic labor-supply framework. It is especially useful for the wiki because it connects schedule flexibility, reservation wages, and estimation strategy in one place.

Short summary

Chen, Ding, List, and Mogstad combine a natural field experiment at Uber with high-frequency panel data to estimate a dynamic labor-supply model. They recover reservation wages, show strong heterogeneity in labor-supply elasticities across people and over time, and estimate workers' willingness to pay for schedule flexibility. They also show that static approaches mismeasure both reservation wages and elasticities in this setting.

The paper matters for discrete scheduling because it studies a labor market with an unusually dense choice set over when to work. In that environment, workers can re-time labor supply at a fine margin. That makes it a powerful contrast to older papers where hours adjustment mainly comes through job changes or fixed-hours offers.

What to carry forward

  • Reservation wages vary across workers and within workers over time.
  • Dynamic models can matter a lot when scheduling choices are frequent and lumpy.
  • The structure of the scheduling menu affects both behavior and measurement.

Caution

This is still evidence from a platform setting with very unusual flexibility. It is best read as a ceiling case for schedule discretion rather than a generic description of employment relationships in standard firms.
