Why it matters

This is the classic ETI benchmark. It changed the conversation by arguing that the policy-relevant response to taxation is not just hours worked, but taxable income more broadly.

Short summary

Using a Treasury panel around the 1986 U.S. tax reform, Feldstein estimates a very large taxable-income response to changes in marginal tax rates. The substantive point is as important as the numeric one: taxable income reflects labor supply, effort, compensation form, avoidance, and other margins that matter for revenue and efficiency.

For the wiki, this paper matters because later ETI debates are basically a long argument about how much of Feldstein's object is causal, how stable it is across settings, and how directly it can be mapped back into compensated and uncompensated labor-supply elasticities.

What to carry forward

  • Taxable income can be more policy-relevant than hours alone.
  • Large tax responses imply large efficiency costs if the estimate is causal.
  • Once taxable income becomes the object, identification and interpretation become harder.

Caution

The headline elasticity is controversial. Many later papers argue that the original design is sensitive to specification choices, income trends, and mean reversion.
