How Elastic is the Labour Supply of Female Migrants Relative to the Labour Supply of Female Natives?

A strong reminder that demographic heterogeneity in labor supply is not just about men versus women; migrant-native differences can be large and policy-relevant.

Why it matters

A lot of the classic literature talks about women, men, married couples, and second earners. This paper shows why that is not enough. Migrant status and integration also shape labor-supply responsiveness.

Short summary

Using data from the German Socio-Economic Panel and a grouping instrumental-variable estimator, Fendel estimates labor-supply elasticities for female migrants and female natives. Female migrants living with a partner show lower own- and cross-wage elasticities than comparable natives, and the elasticities for non-Western female migrants are especially weak or insignificant. The paper also finds that elasticities rise with time since migration, linking responsiveness to labor-market integration.

The bigger lesson is that demographic differences in elasticities are not just household-structure facts. They can also reflect language, institutions, social norms, labor-market access, and barriers to adjustment.

What to carry forward

  • Migrant-native differences belong in the elasticity conversation.
  • Elasticity can move with integration and constraints, not only preferences.
  • Demographic heterogeneity is wider than the canonical husband-wife framework.

Caution

This is one country and one subgroup of the labor market. The paper is best read as evidence that migration status can matter a lot, not as the last word on migrant elasticities everywhere.
