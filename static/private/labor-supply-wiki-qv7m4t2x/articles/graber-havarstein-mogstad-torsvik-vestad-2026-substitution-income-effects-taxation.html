Why it matters

This is one of the clearest recent papers on a recurring confusion in tax-and-labor-supply work: an elasticity of taxable income estimate is not automatically the labor-supply elasticity policymakers actually want.

Short summary

Graber, Håvarstein, Mogstad, Torsvik, and Vestad first show when a common class of ETI estimands can be interpreted causally in the presence of heterogeneity. They then study a Norwegian tax reform and find that ETI rises strongly with income, so average elasticities hide substantial distributional variation.

The second contribution is the one that makes this paper especially useful for the wiki. The authors show how to bound, and in some cases point identify, compensated and uncompensated labor-supply elasticities by combining tax-reform evidence with separate evidence on income effects from lottery winnings. That move lets them distinguish substitution effects from income effects much more cleanly than the older ETI literature usually could.

What to carry forward

  • ETI is not the same object as the labor-supply elasticity unless additional conditions hold.
  • Heterogeneity across the income distribution is first-order, not a side detail.
  • Separate evidence on unearned-income shocks can discipline the income-effect side of tax-response estimates.

Caution

This is still a working paper, and the empirical setting is a specific Norwegian reform. It is best read as a frontier organizing paper rather than a final universal benchmark.
