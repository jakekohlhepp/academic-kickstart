Why it matters

This paper is useful because it treats leave policy as part of the labor-supply problem itself. Workers are not deciding in an institutional vacuum. They are deciding under rules that determine how costly it is to step out of work and return.

Short summary

Han, Ruhm, and Waldfogel study parental leave policies and show that leave rules affect both leave-taking and employment outcomes. Moderate leave access can strengthen job continuity by making temporary exit less costly, while very long leaves may have weaker or more complicated effects on later employment.

For the wiki, the main lesson is that labor supply around childbirth is structured by institutional timing rules. Participation is not just a choice between work and home. It is also a choice about whether the labor market relationship can be preserved through interruption.

What to carry forward

  • Leave policy changes the dynamic structure of labor supply.
  • Institutions can affect retention as well as immediate participation.
  • The design of reentry rules matters, not just the existence of benefits.

Caution

Cross-country institutional comparisons are informative but never perfectly clean. The paper is strongest as evidence that leave design matters, even if precise magnitudes depend on broader policy context.
