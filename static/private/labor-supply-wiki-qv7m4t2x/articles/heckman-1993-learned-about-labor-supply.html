American Economic Review - 1993

What Has Been Learned About Labor Supply in the Past Twenty Years?

A Heckman synthesis that is especially useful for thinking about why heterogeneous individual elasticities do not aggregate mechanically.

Authors
James J. Heckman

Why it matters

This paper is a useful bridge between early labor-supply estimation and later concerns about aggregation. It warns against treating a single micro elasticity as though it automatically scales into the relevant population or policy object.

How to use it in this wiki

  • Read it next to the MaCurdy, Pencavel, and Blundell-MaCurdy entries for the older elasticity foundations.
  • Use it as the conceptual starting point for the later aggregation papers by Attanasio et al., Kneip et al., and Heathcote-Storesletten-Violante.
  • Keep the main lesson close: heterogeneity in preferences, constraints, participation, and wages changes what an "average" elasticity means.
