Why it matters

This is one of the classic papers for people who want a cleaner read on income effects. The basic appeal is simple: lottery prizes generate plausibly exogenous variation in unearned income.

Short summary

Imbens, Rubin, and Sacerdote survey lottery players and compare labor-market outcomes across prize sizes. They find modest labor-supply responses for smaller prizes and larger negative responses for bigger unearned-income shocks. The design is valuable because the income shock is not itself chosen by the worker.

For the current wiki build, this paper matters because newer tax papers such as Graber et al. use lottery evidence precisely to pin down the income-effect side of labor supply. That makes this article a key piece of the bridge between wealth shocks and tax elasticities.

What to carry forward

  • Randomized unearned-income shocks are powerful for identifying income effects.
  • Income effects may be present without implying large participation collapses.
  • Lottery evidence can complement tax-reform evidence instead of competing with it.

Caution

Lottery evidence identifies responses to wealth shocks, not everything that matters in a tax reform. Mapping those estimates into tax objects still requires structure and judgment.
