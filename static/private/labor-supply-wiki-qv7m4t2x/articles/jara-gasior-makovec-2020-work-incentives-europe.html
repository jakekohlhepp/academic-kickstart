Why it matters

This page brings the wiki forward into contemporary policy measurement. It is less about a single elasticity parameter and more about how tax-benefit systems create different wedges across households.

Short summary

Jara, Gasior, and Makovec use microsimulation and representative household data to compare participation tax rates and marginal effective tax rates across the European Union and the United Kingdom. The paper shows that incentive patterns vary sharply across countries and across household types, especially for second earners, families with children, and lower-income groups.

The paper is helpful because it keeps both margins in view. Some households are mainly facing a participation problem, while others face weak incentives to increase hours once they are already working.

What to carry forward

  • Work incentives are highly distributional.
  • Extensive and intensive margins can point to different policy bottlenecks.
  • Modern policy analysis often maps incentive wedges before jumping to causal claims.

Caution

This is comparative microsimulation, not a direct causal labor-supply estimate. It is best read as a map of incentive environments and population exposure.
