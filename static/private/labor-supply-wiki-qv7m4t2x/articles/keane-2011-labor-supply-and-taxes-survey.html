Labor Supply and Taxes: A Survey

A long-form survey arguing that tax-and-labor-supply estimates depend heavily on whether researchers allow for fixed costs, household structure, and dynamic human-capital effects.

Why it matters

If you want one modern survey that takes both male and female labor-supply literatures seriously and keeps the tax angle front and center, this is the one.

Short summary

Keane reviews the literature by separating models for men and women and by emphasizing the modeling decisions that most change estimated elasticities. For men, he focuses on the gap between static models and dynamic models with savings and human capital. For women, he emphasizes participation costs, fertility, marriage, and other margins that make extensive responses central.

This page is especially useful next to ETI papers because it is a reminder that "small" and "large" elasticities often come from different underlying models of behavior. It also helps explain why long-run tax effects can be larger than short-run within-period responses.

What to carry forward

  • Dynamic human-capital channels can make tax effects look larger over longer horizons.
  • Extensive-margin and family-structure choices are central for many female labor-supply estimates.
  • Survey work can be conceptually demanding because the main disagreement is often about model structure, not just data quality.

Caution

Keane takes strong interpretive positions about what the literature implies. That is part of what makes the survey useful, but it also means readers should compare it with more neutral review pieces.
