Changing Work and Work-Family Conflict: Evidence from the Work, Family, and Health Network

A group-randomized workplace intervention showing that organizational rules and supervisor behavior change the time costs workers face from employment.

Why it matters

This paper is especially useful because it moves beyond case-study interpretation. It shows in a stronger design that workplace rules and supervisor practices can change how burdensome employment feels for workers and families.

Short summary

Kelly and coauthors study the Work, Family, and Health Network intervention, which increased schedule control and supervisor support in an information-technology setting. The intervention reduced work-family conflict and improved employees' sense of time control. The main implication for labor supply is that employer-side rules affect the effective cost of supplying labor by changing predictability, autonomy, and the compatibility of work with family responsibilities.

This belongs in the wiki because it treats the organization itself as part of the model. Workers do not meet a wage in the abstract. They meet a bundle of rules, expectations, and supervisory practices.

What to carry forward

  • Supervisor support and scheduling norms are part of the labor-supply environment.
  • Organizational interventions can alter the utility cost of work.
  • Adjacent fields often study labor-supply constraints without using elasticity language.

Caution

Like the 2011 paper, this article is stronger on mechanism than on a standard elasticity object. Its value is in showing that organizational time regimes are empirically real and causally important.
