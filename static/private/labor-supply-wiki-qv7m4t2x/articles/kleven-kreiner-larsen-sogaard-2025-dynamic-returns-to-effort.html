Micro versus Macro Labor Supply Elasticities: The Role of Dynamic Returns to Effort

A recent frontier paper showing that long-run earnings responses to taxes can be much larger than short-run responses because effort pays off through delayed job ladders and mobility.

Why it matters

This is one of the strongest recent papers for the current frontier of the elasticity literature. It shows why short-run tax responses can miss longer-run adjustments that work through promotions, job switching, and dynamic returns to effort.

Short summary

Kleven, Kreiner, Larsen, and Sogaard build and estimate a framework in which the payoff to effort is delayed rather than immediate. Using Danish administrative data and quasi-experimental variation tied to tax incentives, they find long-run earnings elasticities around 0.5, substantially above short-run elasticities around 0.2.

The paper matters because it sharpens a major current gap in the literature: many credible empirical designs identify local and short-run responses, while policy often cares about longer-run adjustments that unfold through career dynamics. This is a good example of a paper trying to bridge that gap rather than choosing one side of the micro-versus-macro debate.

What to carry forward

  • Dynamic returns to effort can make long-run elasticities much larger than short-run elasticities.
  • Job mobility and promotion channels matter for tax-response measurement.
  • A design can be causally credible and still miss the policy-relevant horizon if the adjustment is delayed.

Caution

This is very strong evidence on an important margin, but it is tied to Danish administrative data and to earnings responses among workers whose careers can move through job ladders. It should expand the benchmark set, not collapse the whole literature into one estimate.
