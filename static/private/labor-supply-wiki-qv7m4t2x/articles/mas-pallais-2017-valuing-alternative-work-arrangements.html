Valuing Alternative Work Arrangements

Why it matters

This is the cleanest starting point for the Mas-Pallais literature. It moves the discussion away from treating hours as a single scalar and toward treating jobs as bundles of schedule, location, predictability, and employer discretion.

Short summary

Mas and Pallais run a field experiment in a national call-center hiring process and estimate willingness to pay for alternative work arrangements relative to a standard weekday office job. They find that most workers are not willing to pay much for flexible scheduling per se, but they are willing to give up a great deal to avoid employer discretion over scheduling on short notice. They also estimate a positive willingness to pay for working from home.

The paper matters because it reframes labor supply in package space. The key object is not just the wage for an hour of work. It is the wage attached to a schedule-and-amenity bundle. That is exactly why this paper has become central for hours mismatch, scheduling policy, and organizational design.

What to carry forward

  • Predictability and worker control are distinct from generic flexibility.
  • Jobs differ in nonpecuniary ways that strongly shape labor supply.
  • Hours mismatch is often a mismatch in job package design, not just a mismatch in hours level.

Caution

The evidence comes from a specific labor market and application setting. The design is powerful, but the estimated willingness to pay should not be read as a universal schedule valuation for all workers or occupations.
