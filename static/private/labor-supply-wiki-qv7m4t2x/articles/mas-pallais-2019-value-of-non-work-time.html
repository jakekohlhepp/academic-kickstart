Labor Supply and the Value of Non-Work Time: Experimental Estimates from the Field

A companion Mas-Pallais field experiment estimating how the marginal value of non-work time changes with hours, making labor supply look steep at some hours and flatter at others.

Why it matters

This paper is important because it does not just value job amenities. It directly estimates the value of non-work time over different hours bundles, giving a more shape-rich picture of labor supply than a single constant elasticity.

Short summary

Mas and Pallais offer job applicants randomized wage-hours bundles and recover the marginal value of non-work time as hours increase. They find that labor supply is highly elastic at low hours and becomes more inelastic at higher hours. For unemployed applicants, the opportunity cost of a full-time job relative to leisure, household production, and other non-work activities is substantial.

This matters for hours mismatch because it shows why the same worker may look willing to add a few hours but highly unwilling to move to a full-time schedule. The relevant labor-supply curve is not constant across the hours range.

What to carry forward

  • Labor supply curvature matters.
  • The value of non-work time is not constant over the hours distribution.
  • Moving from part-time to full-time can be a much bigger margin than moving within part-time work.

Caution

The paper identifies a powerful experimental object, but it still comes from a particular hiring environment. The shape of the non-work-time schedule may differ in occupations with very different fixed costs or amenities.
