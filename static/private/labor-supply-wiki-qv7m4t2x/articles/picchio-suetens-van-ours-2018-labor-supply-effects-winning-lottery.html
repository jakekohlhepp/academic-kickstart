Labor Supply Effects of Winning a Lottery

A useful complementary lottery paper showing that sizeable wealth shocks mainly reduce earnings through hours rather than large employment exits.

Why it matters

This paper is useful because it helps discipline how we think about the margin of adjustment under wealth shocks. The response is not mainly mass withdrawal from work.

Short summary

Using Dutch State Lottery winners, Picchio, Suetens, and van Ours find that lottery prizes reduce labor earnings but have little effect on employment status. The interpretation is that wealth shocks show up more through fewer hours than through leaving the labor force altogether, with stronger effects for younger single people without children.

That is a nice complement to the other lottery papers in this cluster. It suggests that income effects can be meaningful even when the extensive margin barely moves, which matters for how newer tax papers translate unearned-income evidence into labor-supply elasticities.

What to carry forward

  • Income effects may work mainly through hours rather than participation.
  • Demographic composition can shape the size of wealth-shock responses.
  • Multiple lottery papers are useful because they illuminate different margins.

Caution

As with other lottery studies, the external validity to tax reforms is not automatic. The paper is best used as one more clean source of evidence on income effects.
