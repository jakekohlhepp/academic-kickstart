Theme page

Discrete scheduling and labor supply

A theme page on labor supply when workers choose among fixed shifts, job packages, or on-off work opportunities rather than a perfectly smooth hours margin.

Why this theme matters

A lot of labor-supply theory starts from a smooth hours margin. But many workers do not face that problem. They face fixed shifts, minimum-hours packages, costly job changes, or discrete moments when they can choose whether to work.

The old insight

The classic hours-constraints literature already points in this direction.

  • Labor Supply Preferences, Hours Constraints, and Hours-Wage Trade-Offs shows that wage-hours packages matter for job choice.
  • Labor Supply, Hours Constraints and Job Mobility shows that hours adjustment often happens when workers move between jobs, not within a job.
  • The Effect of Hours Constraints on Labor Supply Estimates explains why measured elasticities are biased when desired schedules are unavailable.
  • Labor Supply with Hours Restrictions puts the idea into a structural model with fixed-hours offers.

The modern extension

Recent work keeps the same basic issue but studies it in new settings.

  • Valuing Alternative Work Arrangements shows that workers care a lot about predictable, worker-controlled schedules.
  • Labor Supply and the Value of Non-Work Time shows why moving between low and high hours bundles is not a small continuous adjustment.
  • The Value of Flexible Work: Evidence from Uber Drivers studies a setting where workers can choose from a very dense schedule menu.
  • Reservation Wages and Workers' Valuation of Job Flexibility: Evidence from a Natural Field Experiment shows how dynamic modeling changes what we infer about reservation wages and elasticities when scheduling is highly flexible.

The main connection to labor supply

Discrete scheduling changes the interpretation of elasticities. A low observed response to wages can reflect a coarse schedule menu, employer control over timing, or adjustment costs between jobs. A high response can partly reflect unusually fine schedule choice rather than unusually elastic underlying preferences.

That is why this theme belongs next to Adjustment Costs, Firm Responses, and Micro vs. Macro Labor Supply Elasticities: Evidence from Danish Tax Records. The key issue is not just preferences. It is how the feasible choice set is organized.

Relation to hours mismatch

This page is a close companion to hours mismatch and alternative work arrangements. Hours mismatch is often the symptom. Discrete scheduling is one of the underlying mechanisms.

Frontier theory note

As of April 16, 2026, Discrete Choice, Complete Markets, and Equilibrium is included here as a frontier theory reference rather than a standalone article page, because this wiki reserves article pages for peer-reviewed research. It is useful because it shows how discrete choice settings can still be placed inside a general-equilibrium and welfare framework.
