Theme page

Estimation strategies and relative credibility

A practical guide to what different labor-supply designs estimate, what they do well, and where their credibility is strongest or weakest.

There is no single best method

Different designs answer different questions. The credibility ranking depends on whether you care most about local causal identification, long-run dynamic behavior, or counterfactual policy simulation.

A useful way to sort the literature

  1. An Empirical Model of Labor Supply in a Life-Cycle Setting is a classic panel-data life-cycle approach. It is valuable when the goal is intertemporal substitution, but it leans on stronger structure and measurement assumptions.
  2. Family Labor Supply with Taxes represents structural tax-and-household modeling. These models are powerful for counterfactual policy exercises, but credibility depends heavily on functional-form and participation assumptions.
  3. Changes in the Labor Supply Behavior of Married Women: 1980-2000 is a good example of careful reduced-form robustness work using repeated cross-sections.
  4. Bounds on Elasticities With Optimization Frictions is important because it changes how we interpret observed responses when people do not optimize frictionlessly.
  5. The Elasticity of Taxable Income with Respect to Marginal Tax Rates: A Critical Review is essential for understanding when a tax-response estimate can be treated as a sufficient statistic and when it cannot.
  6. The Elasticity of Taxable Income: A Meta-Regression Analysis is a useful calibration check because it shows how much design choice and selective reporting move estimates around.
  7. Micro versus Macro Labor Supply Elasticities: The Role of Dynamic Returns to Effort shows how a highly credible quasi-experimental design can still matter most when it is tied to the right long-run mechanism.
  8. Reservation Wages and Workers' Valuation of Job Flexibility is useful because it shows how dynamic modeling and high-frequency choices can make static estimators misleading even in a very data-rich setting.
  9. The Wage Elasticity of Labour Supply: A Synthesis of Empirical Estimates and Own-Wage Labor Supply Elasticities are review pieces that help calibrate the literature rather than identify one clean causal parameter.

Relative credibility, stated plainly

  • Quasi-experimental designs are usually strongest for local causal credibility.
  • Structural models are usually strongest for policy counterfactuals, but only under stronger maintained assumptions.
  • Panel life-cycle estimates are useful for dynamic questions, but they can be sensitive to wage measurement and model structure.
  • Reviews and meta-analyses are strongest for triangulation and external benchmarking, not for standalone identification.

A missing layer: the organization filters the response

Your notes push on a real issue in this literature. Even a causally careful estimate may not map cleanly to the policy object if firms reshape job packages, task assignments, promotion paths, or amenities in response to the same shock. That means firm-level elasticities, market-level elasticities, and policy-relevant long-run elasticities need not coincide.

Adjustment Costs, Firm Responses, and Micro vs. Macro Labor Supply Elasticities, Micro versus Macro Labor Supply Elasticities: The Role of Dynamic Returns to Effort, and the theme page on organizations and firm-side labor supply are the clearest places in the current wiki where that problem shows up.

The recurring trap

Researchers often argue past each other because they are implicitly comparing different objects: short-run versus long-run responses, hours versus participation, local reform effects versus broad policy counterfactuals, firm-level versus market-level elasticities, or reduced-form elasticities versus structural ones. A credible design can still answer the wrong elasticity question for the policy at hand.

The new companion page on taxable income, substitution, and income effects is the most direct place in the wiki where this "wrong object" problem gets unpacked for tax-response papers.

Model-side companion

For the model structure underneath these estimates, use the new page on utility functions and dynamic vs. static models. That page is about what workers are assumed to maximize, what state variables carry over time, and why those assumptions change the elasticity object.
