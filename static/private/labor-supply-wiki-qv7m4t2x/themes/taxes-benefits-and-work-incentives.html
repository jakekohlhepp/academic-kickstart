Theme page

Taxes, benefits, and work incentives

A policy-facing theme page on wedges, margins, and why tax-benefit design shows up so often in labor supply research.

Why this theme matters

Much of the labor-supply literature becomes concrete only when it is attached to taxes and benefits. Policy creates wedges, nonlinear budget sets, participation penalties, and incentives to change hours.

A useful way to organize the papers

  • Family Labor Supply with Taxes is the classic household-tax starting point.
  • Are Micro and Macro Labor Supply Elasticities Consistent? tells you which elasticity concepts matter for policy.
  • Adjustment Costs, Firm Responses, and Micro vs. Macro Labor Supply Elasticities shows why workers may not move smoothly when policy changes.
  • Bounds on Elasticities With Optimization Frictions shows why welfare and measurement both change when those frictions are real.
  • Substitution and Income Effects of Labor Income Taxation is the new bridge paper if you want ETI estimates translated back into substitution effects, income effects, and actual labor-supply elasticities.
  • The Elasticity of Taxable Income: Evidence and Implications is a core ETI benchmark when the question becomes how taxable income, not just hours, responds to tax reforms.
  • Work incentives at the extensive and intensive margin in Europe gives a newer cross-country policy map.
  • Own-Wage Labor Supply Elasticities helps you place newer estimates in the wider literature.

The recurring lesson

Policy often works through different margins for different groups. Second earners, lower-income households, and workers facing lumpy job offers can all see very different incentive environments even under the same formal tax system.

If you want the ETI side of this theme organized directly, use the companion page on taxable income, substitution, and income effects.

For the optimal-tax version of the same question, use public finance and optimal taxation. For notches, kinks, salience, timing, and earnings-test frictions, use bunching and tax-policy elasticities.
